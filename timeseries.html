Time Series & Autoregressive Models

How we describe a quantity that evolves in time, why we must make it "stationary" first, and how the simplest model — AR(1) — captures whether a series trends or reverts.

What a time series is

What it is

A sequence of observations of one quantity recorded over time — \(X(t_1),X(t_2),\dots\) — where the order matters.

Why it matters

Prices, returns, rates, GDP — almost all financial data is a time series. To forecast or explain it we need models that respect that today depends on yesterday. The "lag" \(\tau\) (gap between observations) is the basic unit.

How it works

We summarise a series with its mean \(\mathbb E[X]\) and variance/volatility \(\mathrm{Var}[X]\), then go further by asking how it depends on its own past. Unlike information theory, you can't reshuffle the observations — order is information.

Analogy

A time series is a diary, not a bag of words. Reading the entries in order tells a story (Monday's mood affects Tuesday's); shuffling them destroys the meaning.

Stationarity

What it is

A series is stationary if its statistical character (mean, variance, how it correlates with its past) doesn't drift over time.

Why it matters

Models assume the rules stay fixed. Fit a model to a drifting (non-stationary) series and you get spurious relationships and unreliable parameters. Raw prices are non-stationary (they trend); returns usually are stationary — which is why we model returns, not prices.

How it works

Strict stationarity: the entire joint distribution is shift-invariant: \(F(X_{t_1+\tau},\dots)=F(X_{t_1},\dots)\) for all \(\tau\). Too strong in practice, so we use weak stationarity:

  • (a) constant mean: \(E[X_t]=\mu\) for all \(t\);
  • (b) autocovariance depends only on the lag: \(\mathrm{Cov}(X_t,X_{t+\tau})=\gamma(\tau)\).

Threats: trend, seasonality, heteroskedasticity (changing volatility — the "H" in GARCH). Fixes: difference the series, take returns, remove trend/seasonality, take logs. Test with the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test or just eyeball it.

Analogy

Stationary = a fair die that stays the same die every roll. If someone keeps secretly reweighting the die (trend) or swapping it at noon (seasonality), any statistics you gather are meaningless.

Worked example

EUR/USD price wanders with no fixed mean → non-stationary (ADF p > 0.05). Take daily returns → roughly constant mean ≈ 0 and stable variance → weakly stationary, ready to model.

Autocorrelation (ACF)

What it is

The correlation of a series with a lagged copy of itself — how much today's value tells you about the value \(\tau\) steps later.

Why it matters

The ACF is the X-ray of a time series: it reveals hidden structure (trends, cycles, mean-reversion) invisible in the raw plot, and it's the foundation of autoregressive modelling.

How it works
\[ \rho_\tau=\mathrm{Cor}(X_t,X_{t+\tau})=\frac{E[(X_t-\mu)(X_{t+\tau}-\mu)]}{\sigma^2} \]

(Assuming stationarity, so the mean μ and variance σ² are the same at every \(t\).) Plotting \(\rho_\tau\) against \(\tau\) is the ACF; bars outside the confidence band signal real structure.

Analogy

The ACF is an echo test: shout into a canyon and listen. A strong echo at a fixed delay (e.g. every 10 steps) reveals a repeating pattern you couldn't see by just looking at the noise.

Worked example

A series \(X_t=\varepsilon_t+\sin(2\pi t/10)\) looks like noise, but its ACF shows a clear spike every 10 lags — exposing the hidden cycle. Positive ACF at lag 1 ⇒ trending; negative ⇒ counter-trending (mean-reverting).

Autoregressive models

How it works

Model the series as a function of its own past plus noise:

\[ \text{AR}(p):\ X_t=c+\sum_{i=1}^p\varphi_i X_{t-i}+\varepsilon_t,\quad \text{MA}(q):\ X_t=\mu+\varepsilon_t+\sum_{i=1}^q\theta_i\varepsilon_{t-i} \]

ARMA(p,q) combines both. AR uses past values; MA uses past shocks. Choosing \(p,q\) (and comparing models) uses criteria like AIC \(=2p-2\ln L\) and BIC \(=p\ln n-2\ln L\) — both reward fit (\(\ln L\)) and penalise complexity (\(p\)).

Analogy

An AR model is "tomorrow looks like a fraction of today, plus a surprise". The coefficient \(\varphi\) is how much memory the series has; \(\varepsilon\) is the daily surprise.

AR(1) — the analytic case

How it works

With \(p=1\): \(X_t=c+\varphi_1 X_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t\), noise \(\varepsilon_t\sim N(0,\sigma_\varepsilon^2)\). Stationarity lets us solve everything in closed form:

\[ \mu=\frac{c}{1-\varphi_1},\qquad \mathrm{Var}(X_t)=\frac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{1-\varphi_1^2}\ (\Rightarrow |\varphi_1|<1),\qquad \varphi_1=\rho_1 \]
Derivation Mean, variance and \(\varphi_1=\rho_1\)

Mean: take expectations: \(E[X_t]=c+\varphi_1E[X_{t-1}]\). Stationarity ⇒ \(E[X_t]=E[X_{t-1}]=\mu\), so \(\mu=c+\varphi_1\mu\Rightarrow\mu=\frac{c}{1-\varphi_1}\). Normalise \(c=0\Rightarrow\mu=0\).

Variance: \(\mathrm{Var}(X_t)=\varphi_1^2\mathrm{Var}(X_{t-1})+\sigma_\varepsilon^2\) (noise independent). Stationarity ⇒ \(V=\varphi_1^2V+\sigma_\varepsilon^2\Rightarrow V=\frac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{1-\varphi_1^2}\). Positive variance forces \(\varphi_1^2<1\).

Lag-1 autocov: \(\gamma_1=E[X_tX_{t+1}]=E[X_t(\varphi_1X_t+\varepsilon_{t+1})]=\varphi_1\mathrm{Var}(X_t)\). Divide by the variance: the lag-1 autocorrelation \(\rho_1=\varphi_1\). So the single parameter is the lag-1 autocorrelation. \(\;\square\)

Interpretation: \(\varphi_1>0\) ⇒ trending (momentum); \(\varphi_1<0\) ⇒ counter-trending (mean-reversion); \(\varphi_1=1\) ⇒ a random walk (non-stationary, perfect fit in-sample). Higher orders have no closed form — estimate by maximum likelihood.

📈What this shows: top — an AR(1) path; bottom — its ACF. φ₁ near +1 = smooth trending wander (slowly-decaying ACF, a near random-walk at φ₁=1); φ₁ near 0 = pure noise (no autocorrelation); φ₁ < 0 = jagged mean-reversion (alternating-sign ACF).
Analogy

φ₁ is a memory dial. At 1, the series never forgets (drifts forever — random walk). At 0, total amnesia (white noise). Negative, it's contrarian — overshoots then springs back.

Examiner might ask Time-series probes
Why model returns rather than prices?
Prices are non-stationary (they trend, no fixed mean/variance), which breaks model assumptions and produces spurious results. Returns are (approximately) stationary, so estimation is reliable.
What does the AR(1) coefficient tell you?
It equals the lag-1 autocorrelation. Positive ⇒ momentum/trending; negative ⇒ mean-reversion; magnitude near 1 ⇒ long memory; =1 ⇒ random walk (unit root, non-stationary).
Why must \(|\varphi_1|<1\)?
Otherwise the variance \(\sigma_\varepsilon^2/(1-\varphi_1^2)\) is non-positive/infinite — the series isn't stationary; shocks accumulate without bound.
AIC vs BIC?
Both trade fit against complexity; BIC's penalty (\(p\ln n\)) is harsher for large samples, so it favours simpler models. Use them to pick \(p,q\) without overfitting.
One-line story for the exam "A time series is ordered data; we make it stationary (usually by taking returns) so its statistics are stable, read its structure from the autocorrelation function, and model it autoregressively. In AR(1) the single coefficient is the lag-1 autocorrelation — positive means trending, negative means reverting, one means a random walk."
