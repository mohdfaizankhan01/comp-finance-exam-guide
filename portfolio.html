Portfolio Construction (MPT)

Markowitz's big idea: by combining imperfectly-correlated assets you can cut risk without sacrificing return. The maths turns "diversification" into an optimisation.

Why build portfolios

What it is

A portfolio is a collection of assets. Modern Portfolio Theory (Markowitz, 1952) says the right way to choose weights is to trade off expected return against risk (variance), exploiting how assets move together.

Why it matters

It's the foundational framework of asset management (Nobel Prize, 1990). It made risk a quantity you engineer, not just endure — and "don't put all your eggs in one basket" became a precise, optimisable statement.

How it works

Robustness comes from diversification: pick assets of different nature so that one's bad day is offset by another's good day, while you keep the returns of both. "Robust" can mean max return/risk (Sharpe ratio), min drawdown, or fast recovery, depending on the investor.

Analogy

Selling both sunscreen and umbrellas. Each alone is seasonal and volatile; together, rain or shine, your revenue is steadier — without lowering your average. That smoothing is diversification.

Expected return & variance of a portfolio

How it works

With weights \(w_i\) (summing to 1), the portfolio return is the weighted sum, and its expectation is linear:

\[ R_p=\sum_i w_i R_i,\qquad E[R_p]=\sum_i w_i\,E[R_i] \]

But the variance is not a simple weighted sum — cross terms (covariances) appear:

\[ \mathrm{Var}(R_p)=\sigma_p^2=\sum_i\sum_j w_i w_j\,\mathrm{Cov}(R_i,R_j)=\mathbf{w}^{\mathsf T}\mathbf{C}\,\mathbf{w} \]
  • C covariance matrix (\(C_{ii}=\sigma_i^2\)) · w weight vector. The covariances are what diversification acts on.
Derivation The \(N=2\) variance expanded

Using \(\mathrm{Var}(aX+bY)=a^2\mathrm{Var}X+b^2\mathrm{Var}Y+2ab\,\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)\):

\[ \sigma_p^2=w_1^2\sigma_1^2+w_2^2\sigma_2^2+2w_1w_2\,\rho_{12}\sigma_1\sigma_2, \]

since \(\mathrm{Cov}(R_1,R_2)=\rho_{12}\sigma_1\sigma_2\). The cross term is negative when \(\rho_{12}<0\) — that's risk reduction in algebra.

Analogy

Returns average like mixing paint (predictable blend). Risk is trickier: two equally "shaky" assets can combine into something calmer than either, if their shakes tend to cancel.

Worked example

Two assets, \(\sigma_1=\sigma_2=0.10\), \(w_1=w_2=0.5\). If \(\rho_{12}=0\): \(\sigma_p=\sqrt{0.25\cdot0.01+0.25\cdot0.01}= \sqrt{0.005}=0.0707\) — already below 0.10 with the same expected return. Free risk reduction.

Correlation — the engine of diversification

How it works
\[ \rho_{ij}=\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(R_i,R_j)}{\sigma_i\sigma_j}\in[-1,1] \]

For two equal-risk, equal-weight assets the portfolio volatility depends entirely on \(\rho\):

ρ₁₂σ_pMeaning
+10.100no benefit — assets move identically
+0.50.087some smoothing

−0.50.050strong offset
−10.000risk fully cancelled (rare!)
📈What this shows: mixing two assets (1 and 2) at every weight, for different correlations. Lower ρ bows the curve further left (less risk for the same return). At ρ = −1 you can engineer zero risk; at ρ = +1 the curve is a straight line — no diversification.
Analogy

Correlation is "do these two dancers step in sync?" If they move oppositely (ρ < 0), their wobbles cancel and the pair looks steady. If they're perfectly in sync (ρ = +1), the pair is just as wobbly as one dancer.

Note / real-world caveat Correlations are not stable — they tend to spike toward +1 in crises (exactly when you need diversification most). 2022 saw stocks and bonds fall together. Quote this to show practical awareness.

Two-asset portfolio

How it works

When the two assets differ in risk/return, varying the weights traces a curve from asset 1 to asset 2 through intermediate blends. Even with \(\rho=-1\), if the risks differ the portfolio risk doesn't hit exactly zero (the perfect cancellation needs matched volatilities). Sweeping all weights and all pairs builds the full opportunity set.

Worked example

\(E[R_1]=0.10,\sigma_1=0.07\); \(E[R_2]=0.20,\sigma_2=0.10\); equal weights → \(E[R_p]=0.15\). With \(\rho=0\), \(\sigma_p=\sqrt{0.5^2(0.07^2)+0.5^2(0.10^2)}=0.061\) — lower than either weighted-average intuition would suggest.

The efficient frontier

What it is

The set of portfolios that give the maximum expected return for each level of risk — the upper-left boundary of all achievable risk/return combinations.

Why it matters

It's the menu of rational choices. Any portfolio below the frontier is dominated — you could get more return for the same risk, or less risk for the same return. Investors should only ever hold frontier portfolios.

How it works

Plot every achievable portfolio in (risk, return) space — you get a cloud bounded by a bullet-shaped envelope. The top edge is the efficient frontier. Of two portfolios A and C at the same return, the lower-risk one (A) wins; of B and C at the same risk, the higher-return one (B) wins.

📈What this shows: a Monte-Carlo cloud of thousands of random 4-asset portfolios. The points that hug the top-left edge form the efficient frontier (highlighted) — best return per unit of risk. Everything inside is dominated.
Analogy

The frontier is the "best deals" line in a car showroom: for each budget (risk), it's the most car (return) you can get. Anything off the line is overpaying.

Computing the frontier

How it works
  • Monte Carlo (brute force): draw random weights summing to 1, plot each portfolio. Simple but slow and inefficient — needs millions of draws to map the high-risk edge as assets grow.
  • Closed-form (Lagrange): minimising \(\mathbf{w}^{\mathsf T}\mathbf{C}\mathbf{w}\) subject to \(\sum w_i=1\) gives an exact analytic solution via \(\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{C}^{-1}(\lambda_1\mathbf{1}+\lambda_2\boldsymbol\mu)\). Elegant, but only enforces the budget constraint (allows shorts/leverage).
  • Numerical optimiser (the practical way): solve \(\min_{\mathbf w}\ \mathbf{w}^{\mathsf T}\mathbf{C}\mathbf{w}-q\,\mathbf{w}^{\mathsf T}\boldsymbol\mu\) with \(\mathbf{1}^{\mathsf T}\mathbf{w}=1\) and bounds \(0\le w\le 1\); sweep the risk-appetite \(q\in[0,\infty)\) to trace the frontier. Handles real-world constraints (caps, no-shorting) easily.

Estimating expected returns is "more art than science" and dominates the result; covariances are usually estimated from history (need at least ~\(N^2/2\) data points to be meaningful). Adding constraints lowers the frontier (you give up some efficiency for realism).

Analogy

Monte Carlo is throwing darts and keeping the best; the optimiser is aiming deliberately. Same target, but aiming wins once the dartboard (number of assets) gets big.

Don't say this "MPT gives the optimal portfolio." It gives the optimal portfolio given your inputs — and the expected-return inputs are guesses. Garbage in, garbage out. Mention input sensitivity.

Risk-free asset & the Capital Market Line

How it works

Add a risk-free asset (a short-term sovereign bond: small return \(r\), ≈ zero risk, ≈ zero correlation). Combining it with a risky portfolio \(P_0\) gives:

\[ E[R_{P_1}]=w_{rf}\,r+(1-w_{rf})E[R_{P_0}],\qquad \sigma_{P_1}=(1-w_{rf})\,\sigma_{P_0} \]
Derivation Why the risk is just \((1-w_{rf})\sigma_{P_0}\)

The risk-free asset has \(\sigma_{rf}=0\) and \(\rho_{rf,P_0}=0\). Plug into the two-asset variance:

\[ \sigma_{P_1}^2=w_{rf}^2\cdot0+(1-w_{rf})^2\sigma_{P_0}^2+2w_{rf}(1-w_{rf})\cdot0\cdot\sigma_{rf}\sigma_{P_0}=(1-w_{rf})^2\sigma_{P_0}^2. \]

Take the square root → \(\sigma_{P_1}=(1-w_{rf})\sigma_{P_0}\). Both return and risk are now linear in \(w_{rf}\) — a straight line in (risk, return) space. \(\;\square\)

Because the combination is a straight line, the best line from \(r\) is the one tangent to the efficient frontier: the Capital Market Line (CML). Every investor holds some mix of the risk-free asset and that single tangency portfolio, scaled to their risk appetite (and leverage).

Analogy

The risk-free asset is a dimmer switch on one ideal "best" portfolio: turn it down (hold cash) for less risk, turn it up past 100% (borrow/leverage) for more — all along one straight, efficient line.

Examiner might ask MPT probes
Where does diversification's benefit come from, mathematically?
The covariance/cross terms in \(\sigma_p^2=\mathbf w^{\mathsf T}\mathbf C\mathbf w\). When \(\rho<1\) the cross term is less than the weighted average of variances, so portfolio risk falls below the weighted-average risk — without lowering expected return.
What is the efficient frontier and why hold only frontier portfolios?
The set maximising return per unit risk. Any interior portfolio is dominated — you could strictly improve return or risk — so a rational investor never holds one.
What does adding a risk-free asset do?
It creates the straight Capital Market Line from \(r\) tangent to the frontier; combinations of cash and the tangency portfolio dominate the curved frontier, so everyone holds the same risky portfolio in different proportions.
Biggest practical weakness of MPT?
Sensitivity to inputs — especially expected returns (hard to forecast) and unstable correlations that jump to +1 in crises, exactly when diversification is needed. Estimation error can dominate the "optimisation".
One-line story for the exam "MPT picks weights to trade expected return against variance. Because portfolio variance \(\mathbf w^{\mathsf T}\mathbf C\mathbf w\) depends on correlations, combining imperfectly-correlated assets cuts risk for free. The best risk/return combos form the efficient frontier; adding a risk-free asset turns it into the straight Capital Market Line — but the answer is only as good as the return estimates fed in."
