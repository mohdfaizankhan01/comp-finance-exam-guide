Data, Moving Averages & Intrinsic Time

Real market data is messy and irregular. Three ways to smooth it — SMA, WMA, EMA — and a radical idea: let the market, not the clock, define time.

Data in real life

What it is

We imagine price data as smooth, continuous and equally spaced. In reality it's a noisy stream of bid/ask quotes arriving at irregular times.

Why it matters

Every model assumes clean inputs. Knowing data is actually non-equidistant, noisy and sometimes discontinuous tells you why we smooth (moving averages) and why naïve discretisation loses information.

How it works

Two handling choices: discretise (sample at fixed intervals — used 99.9% of the time, but misses what happens between ticks) or interpolate (rare; strong assumptions). Either way, physical time feels arbitrary and rigid — a theme revisited by intrinsic time.

Analogy

Expecting a smooth line but getting jagged ticks is like expecting a polished song and hearing the raw, crackly studio recording — full of gaps and noise you must clean up.

Simple Moving Average (SMA)

What it is

The plain average of the last \(n\) observations — slide the window forward each step.

Why it matters

It's the simplest noise filter, the baseline every trader knows. Its flaws (equal weighting, lag) motivate the better averages.

How it works
\[ \text{SMA}(t)=\frac{X_{t-n+1}+\dots+X_t}{n},\qquad \text{SMA}(t{+}1)=\text{SMA}(t)-\frac{X_{t-n+1}}{n}+\frac{X_{t+1}}{n} \]

The update drops the oldest point and adds the newest — but you must keep all \(n\) points in memory. Two flaws: old data counts as much as new; and there's an inherent time lag (bigger \(n\) ⇒ smoother but laggier).

Analogy

Like judging today's mood by the plain average of the last 10 days — a heatwave that ended a week ago still drags the average up as much as today's weather.

Weighted Moving Average (WMA)

How it works

Weight recent points more — linearly, newest gets weight \(n\), oldest gets 1:

\[ \text{WMA}(t)=\frac{1\cdot X_{t-n+1}+2\cdot X_{t-n+2}+\dots+n\cdot X_t}{n+(n-1)+\dots+1},\qquad \sum_{k=1}^n k=\frac{n(n+1)}{2} \]

It fixes the "old = new" flaw, but the update is cumbersome (you must recompute a running total). The newest point's weight is \(\frac{2}{n+1}\) of the total.

Analogy

A teacher who weights recent test scores more than early ones — fairer to your current ability, but more bookkeeping to compute.

Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

What it is

An average whose weights decay exponentially into the past — and which updates with a single, elegant recursion.

Why it matters

It's the practitioner's favourite: smooth, recent-weighted, and needs only the previous value to update — no window to store. It's everywhere (MACD, vol estimates, RSI).

How it works
\[ \text{EMA}(t)=\alpha\,X_t+(1-\alpha)\,\text{EMA}(t-1) \]
Derivation From the infinite weighted sum to the recursion

Start from \(\text{EMA}(t)=\dfrac{X_t+(1-\alpha)X_{t-1}+(1-\alpha)^2X_{t-2}+\dots}{1+(1-\alpha)+(1-\alpha)^2+\dots}\). The denominator is a geometric series summing to \(1/\alpha\). Pull out \(X_t\) and factor \((1-\alpha)\) from the rest — the remaining fraction is exactly \(\text{EMA}(t-1)\):

\[ \text{EMA}(t)=\alpha X_t+(1-\alpha)\text{EMA}(t-1).\;\square \]

Choosing \(\alpha=\frac{2}{n+1}\) makes an EMA's average "age" equal an \(n\)-window SMA's (≈ comparable smoothing). Or set the impact of a point after \(k\) steps to \(\epsilon\): \(\alpha=1-e^{\ln\epsilon/k}\).

  • α the smoothing factor in (0,1): bigger α = more reactive (short lag), smaller α = smoother (long lag).
📈What this shows: a noisy price (grey) and its three smoothings. All three lag behind turns — bigger window / smaller α = smoother but laggier. The EMA reacts fastest for a given smoothness because it weights recent data most.
Analogy

EMA is your opinion of a friend: mostly your view from yesterday, nudged a little by what they did today. Memory fades smoothly — you don't suddenly forget everything 10 days ago like an SMA window does.

Note The unavoidable trade-off: smoother ⇒ laggier. No moving average escapes it. Setting \(\alpha=\frac{2}{n+1}\) makes an EMA comparable to an \(n\)-SMA but they are not identical.

Intrinsic time

What it is

A clock driven by price events rather than the wall clock — time "ticks" only when the price reverses by a chosen amount.

Why it matters

Physical time is arbitrary (when does a "day" start? markets sleep and surge). Important moves can hide between fixed samples. Letting the market set the rhythm makes time adaptive — fast when volatile, slow when calm — and connects naturally to volatility.

How it works

Pick a threshold \(\delta>0\). An event (a directional change) is logged whenever price reverses by \(\delta\) from the last extreme; between events you observe an overshoot. The count of directional changes per unit physical time measures volatility; overshoot length reflects liquidity. A web of empirical scaling laws (e.g. \(\langle\Delta t_{dc}\rangle\sim(\delta/c)^\alpha\)) links intrinsic and physical time.

Analogy

Like measuring a hike by switchbacks climbed instead of minutes elapsed. On a steep, twisty stretch (volatile market) the "switchback clock" races; on a flat path (calm market) it barely moves — a more natural sense of how much has actually happened.

Examiner might ask Averages & intrinsic time
SMA vs EMA — when use which?
SMA is simple and equal-weighted but needs the full window and lags; EMA weights recent data, updates with one previous value (memory-light) and is more responsive for the same smoothness. EMA is usually preferred in practice.
Why does every moving average lag?
Because it's an average of past data; the more you smooth (larger \(n\), smaller \(\alpha\)) the more weight is on older points, so the average trails turning points. Smoothness and lag are inseparable.
What problem does intrinsic time solve?
Physical time is arbitrary and lets events go unnoticed between samples. Intrinsic time samples by price activity, so it's dense when markets are active and connects directly to volatility (count of directional changes).
Derive the EMA recursion in one line.
The exponential-weight numerator over the geometric denominator \(1/\alpha\) factors into \(\alpha X_t+(1-\alpha)\text{EMA}(t-1)\) — current point plus a faded copy of the previous average.
One-line story for the exam "Real data is noisy and irregular, so we smooth it: SMA (equal weights, laggy), WMA (linear weights), and EMA (exponential weights, one-line recursion, memory-light) — all trading smoothness for lag. Intrinsic time goes further, replacing the arbitrary wall clock with a price-event clock that's fast when volatile and slow when calm."
