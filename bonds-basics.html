Bonds — Basics

A bond is a loan with a fixed schedule. Its price is just the present value of that schedule — and price moves inversely to yield.

What a bond is

What it is

A bond is an IOU: the issuer borrows money now, pays periodic coupons, and returns the principal (face value) at maturity, all on a fixed, contractual schedule.

Why it matters

Bonds are how governments and companies fund themselves, and they're the bedrock of portfolios (income, safety, diversification). Their pricing is the purest application of the time value of money, and the "risk-free rate" used everywhere in finance comes from government bonds.

How it works

The structure (coupons, dates, principal) is fixed for life. What floats is the price \(B(t)\), which is the market's verdict on the bond, driven by the yield investors demand — itself a function of the issuer's risk of default (credit risk).

Analogy

A bond is a fixed set of dated cheques. The cheques never change. But how much someone will pay you today for that stack of future cheques changes with interest rates and how much they trust the cheque-writer.

Valuation — discount every cash flow

What it is

The fair price is the sum of every future coupon and the principal, each discounted back to today at the required yield.

Why it matters

It makes "what is this loan worth?" a precise calculation, and it's the formula from which duration, convexity and the whole yield curve follow.

How it works
\[ B(t)=\sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i\,e^{-y\,(t_i-t)} + P\,e^{-y\,(T-t)} \]
  • c_i coupon at time \(t_i\) · P principal (face value) repaid at maturity \(T\)
  • y the required yield (continuous compounding here) · the exponential is the discount factor.

A bond trading above 100 (par) is at a premium; at 100 is at par; below is at a discount.

Analogy

Pricing a bond is weighing each future cheque on a "time scale" that shrinks far-off money, then adding the shrunken amounts. The further away a cheque, the less it weighs today.

Worked example

Coupon \(c=5\), paid twice a year for 2 years, principal 100, required yield \(y=5\%\):

0.555·e^{-0.05·0.5}4.88
1.055·e^{-0.05·1}4.76
1.555·e^{-0.05·1.5}4.64
2.055·e^{-0.05·2}4.52
2.0100 (principal)100·e^{-0.05·2}90.48

\(B=4.88+4.76+4.64+4.52+90.48=\mathbf{109.28}\) → selling at a premium (its 10% annual coupon beats the 5% yield).

Yield & the price–yield relationship

What it is

The yield is the single discount rate that makes the bond's discounted cash flows equal its market price — the bond's internal rate of return.

Why it matters

It's the standard "interest rate" of a bond, and the key fact — price and yield move in opposite directions — explains why rising rates hurt bondholders. This inverse, curved relationship is the seed of duration and convexity.

How it works

Given a market price \(B(t)\), you can't solve for \(y\) in closed form (it's inside several exponentials), so you use a trial-and-error / numerical root-find: guess \(y\), compute \(B\), adjust until it matches. As \(y\) rises, every discount factor \(e^{-y\cdot t}\) shrinks, so \(B\) falls — and the curve is convex (bends), not a straight line.

📈What this shows: price vs yield for the 2-year bond above. It slopes down (higher yield ⇒ lower price) and is convex (curved) — the curvature is exactly what duration approximates and convexity corrects (next lecture). The dot marks par-yield.
Analogy

Yield and price are a see-saw. If newly-issued bonds suddenly pay more (yields up), your old lower-paying bond looks worse, so its price must drop to stay competitive — and vice versa.

Don't say this "If interest rates rise my bond is worth more because it earns more." Your old bond's coupons are fixed — rising rates make it less attractive, so its price falls.

Zero-coupon yield — a common yardstick

What it is

The yield on a bond that pays no coupons — just one lump at maturity. It depends only on maturity, so it's the clean standard for comparing rates.

Why it matters

Two coupon bonds with different schedules have yields that aren't directly comparable. Strip out the coupons and you get the zero-coupon (spot) yield curve — the fundamental object used to discount any cash flow at the right rate for its date.

How it works

In theory the curve rises smoothly with maturity (longer = more risk/uncertainty → higher rate). In practice it can be humped, flat or inverted. Since not all zero-coupon bonds trade, we construct the curve from coupon bonds via the bootstrap.

Analogy

Zero-coupon yields are like quoting every product as a price-per-100g: only once you standardise can you compare a small jar to a big one fairly.

Bootstrap algorithm

What it is

A step-by-step way to peel out zero-coupon rates from the prices of real, coupon-paying bonds, starting at the shortest maturity and working outward.

Why it matters

It's how the discount curve everyone uses is actually built from market data. The trick — using already-known short rates to discount the early coupons of a longer bond — is elegant and very examinable.

How it works

For each bond, in increasing maturity, all earlier cash flows are discounted with rates you've already bootstrapped, leaving the new maturity's rate as the only unknown — then solve for it. Convert quoted rates to continuous compounding as you go.

Worked bootstrap Treasury data → zero curve

Short zeros first. A 3-month bill priced 97.5 (principal 100): quarterly growth \(4\times2.5/97.5=10.256\%\); annualise to continuous: \(r_{0.25}=4\ln(1+0.10256/4)=\mathbf{10.127\%}\). Similarly \(r_{0.5}=10.469\%\), \(r_{1.0}=\ln(1+10/90)=10.536\%\).

Now the 1.5-year, 8% coupon bond (semi-annual → $4) priced 96. Coupons at 0.5 and 1.0 use known rates; only \(r_{1.5}\) is unknown:

\[ 4e^{-0.10469\cdot0.5}+4e^{-0.10536\cdot1}+104\,e^{-r_{1.5}\cdot1.5}=96 \]

Solve: \(e^{-1.5 r_{1.5}}=0.85196\Rightarrow r_{1.5}=\mathbf{10.681\%}\). Repeat for the 2-year bond → \(r_{2.0}=10.808\%\).

Like solving a sudoku in order: each new cell (maturity) has exactly one unknown left once you've filled the earlier ones, so you can always solve it before moving on.

Types, ratings & credit spread

How it works
  • Government (sovereign / "Govies" / Treasuries) vs corporate bonds.
  • The extra yield a corporate pays over the matching government bond is the credit spread: bond yield = sovereign yield + credit spread.
  • Rating agencies grade default risk: S&P AAA→D, Moody's Aaa→C. Lower rating ⇒ higher yield demanded. Ratings can change mid-life, widening or narrowing the spread.
  • Central banks usually carry the best (≈AAA) rating for their country; corporates are rated on activity and balance sheet. Ratings don't include ESG factors.
Analogy

The credit spread is the "risky-borrower surcharge" on a loan: a friend with a shaky job must promise you a higher interest rate than the government would, to compensate for the chance they don't pay you back.

Risks & role in a portfolio

How it works
  • Interest-rate risk: rates up ⇒ price down. Longer-duration bonds are more sensitive (next lecture).
  • Credit risk: the issuer may default; rated by agencies; compensated by yield spread.
  • Inflation risk: inflation erodes real returns (real ≈ nominal − inflation); TIPS / inflation-linked bonds protect against it.
  • Liquidity risk: you may not sell quickly at fair value.
  • Reinvestment risk: coupons/principal may have to be reinvested at lower rates — acute for callable bonds, which issuers redeem early when rates fall.

Role in a portfolio: income, capital preservation, and diversification — bonds often have low/negative correlation with equities (so they cushion equity drawdowns). Caveat: in 2022 rates spiked and stock–bond correlation turned positive, so both fell together.

Analogy

Bonds are the ballast in a portfolio's ship — steady, less exciting, usually keeping you upright when equities lurch. But ballast that shifts (rising rates) can occasionally rock the boat too.

Examiner might ask Bonds basics probes
Why does a bond's price fall when yields rise?
The cash flows are fixed; discounting them at a higher rate shrinks every present value, so the sum (the price) drops. Equivalently, your fixed coupon is now uncompetitive versus new higher-coupon bonds.
What is the bootstrap doing, in one sentence?
Extracting pure zero-coupon (spot) rates from coupon-bond prices, one maturity at a time, by discounting earlier coupons with already-solved rates so each step has a single unknown.
What's the credit spread and what moves it?
The yield premium over the matching sovereign bond, compensating for default risk; it widens on downgrades, recessions, or rising default fears, and narrows when credit conditions improve.
Are government bonds truly risk-free?
Approximately — there's a small but nonzero chance a central bank/government defaults, usually treated as ≈0 for major sovereigns in their own currency. They're the practical proxy for the "risk-free rate".
One-line story for the exam "A bond is a fixed schedule of cash flows; its price is their present value, so price and yield move inversely along a convex curve. We standardise rates via the zero-coupon curve, build it by bootstrapping coupon bonds, add a credit spread for default risk, and hold bonds for income, safety and diversification."
