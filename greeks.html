What the Greeks are

What it is

The Greeks are the partial derivatives of an option's price — each one measures how much the price moves when one input changes and the rest stay fixed.

Why it matters

An option is exposed to many risks at once (price, time, volatility, rates). The Greeks decompose that risk into separate, hedgeable numbers, so you can neutralise the bits you don't want and keep the bit you're paid for.

How it works
GreekSymbolSensitivity to…Derivative
DeltaΔunderlying price \(S\)\(\partial c/\partial S\)
GammaΓDelta itself (curvature)\(\partial^2 c/\partial S^2\)
ThetaΘpassage of time\(\partial c/\partial t\)
Vegaνvolatility \(\sigma\)\(\partial c/\partial\sigma\)
Rhoρinterest rate \(r\)\(\partial c/\partial r\)
Analogy

The Greeks are the car dashboard. Delta is the speedometer (how fast value changes with the underlying), Gamma is the accelerator pedal's sensitivity (how fast the speed itself changes), Theta is the fuel gauge draining with time, Vega is sensitivity to how shaky the road is.

Managing an option's risk

What it is

The choices a writer faces after selling an option: do nothing, fully cover, react crudely, or hedge precisely using the Greeks.

Why it matters

Writing options is profitable but risky. Black–Scholes tells you the option's fair cost to hedge; the writer's job is to actually run that hedge so the premium becomes profit rather than a landmine.

How it works
  • Naked: do nothing. If the option finishes out-of-the-money you keep the whole premium; if not, losses can be huge.
  • Covered: buy the shares upfront. Safe against a price rise, but costly and exposed if the price falls.
  • Stop-loss: hold shares when \(S>K\), sell when \(S<K\). Sounds clever but fails: you always buy a touch high and sell a touch low, and around \(S=K\) you whipsaw. Its cost ≠ the true option cost because cash flows happen at different times and \(S=K\) crossings are ambiguous.
  • Greek hedging: use Delta (and Gamma) to neutralise the risks continuously. This is the right answer.
Analogy

Naked = driving with no insurance. Covered = buying a second car "just in case". Stop-loss = slamming the brakes every time you cross a line (you wear out the brakes and still crash). Greek hedging = adaptive cruise control that constantly trims your speed.

Worked example (the running case)

Write a European call for $300k on 100k shares. \(S_0=49,\ K=50,\ r=5\%,\ \sigma=20\%,\ T=20/52=0.385\). Black–Scholes says the option is worth ≈ $240k. You banked $300k, so $60k of expected profit — if you hedge the residual risk well.

Delta (Δ)

What it is

How much the option price moves for a $1 move in the underlying — the slope of the price-vs-spot curve.

Why it matters

Delta is the first-order price risk and the hedge ratio: it tells you exactly how many shares to hold to offset an option's price exposure. It's also (roughly) the risk-neutral probability of finishing in the money.

How it works
\[ \Delta=\frac{\partial c}{\partial S},\qquad \Delta_{\text{call}}=N(d_1),\qquad \Delta_{\text{put}}=N(d_1)-1 \]
  • Call Delta runs 0→1 (deep OTM→deep ITM); put Delta runs −1→0.
  • A small price change \(\delta S\) changes the option by \(\approx \Delta\,\delta S\) — a linear (first-order) approximation. Options aren't linear, hence "approximately" (Gamma fixes the rest).
📈What this shows: call & put Delta vs spot (\(K=50\)). The call's Delta is an S-shaped climb 0→1; the put's is the same curve shifted down to −1→0. Shorten T or raise σ and the S-curve flattens/steepens around the strike.
Analogy

Delta is the option's speedometer: at Δ = 0.6 the option gains $0.60 for every $1 the stock gains — it's "going 60% as fast" as the stock. Deep in the money it tracks the stock 1-for-1 (Δ→1); far out, it barely moves (Δ→0).

Worked example

Δ = 0.6, stock rises $1 → call rises ≈ $0.60. Hold 1 short call and you're exposed to +0.6 share-equivalents of price risk.

Delta hedging

What it is

Holding Δ shares of the underlying against each option so that, for small price moves, the combined position doesn't change value — it becomes "Delta-neutral".

Why it matters

It's how option writers turn a risky obligation into a (nearly) riskless harvesting of the premium. It's also the practical embodiment of the no-arbitrage pricing argument.

How it works

A \(\delta S\) move changes the option by \(\approx\Delta\,\delta S\). Hold \(\Delta\) of the underlying and the share leg moves the opposite way by the same amount → net ≈ 0.

To buy (long)To sell (short)
Call−Δ_c+Δ_c
Put−Δ_p+Δ_p

Crucially, Δ changes as \(S\) moves and as time passes, so the hedge must be rebalanced continuously — in practice, frequently. This rebalancing cost is what the premium pays for.

Analogy

Like balancing on a seesaw that keeps shifting. You add weight (shares) to cancel the option's tilt, but as the price moves the required weight changes, so you must keep stepping left and right to stay level.

Worked example

Sell a call on 2000 shares at \(S=100\), Δ = 0.6. Buy \(0.6\times2000=1200\) shares. If \(S\to101\), the call value rises by \(0.6\Rightarrow\) you owe $0.6×2000 = $1200 more on the option, but your 1200 shares gain $1×1200 = $1200. Net P&L ≈ 0. ✓

Short-put case: Δ_p = −0.4, sell 0.4×2000 = 800 shares; same cancellation logic.

Examiner might ask Delta & delta hedging
Why must you rebalance a delta hedge?
Because Delta isn't constant — it changes with \(S\) (that's Gamma) and with time. A hedge that's neutral now drifts off-neutral as the market moves, so you re-trade to restore Δ = 0.
What does delta hedging cost, and why isn't it free?
You repeatedly buy high / sell low while chasing Δ (because of Gamma), plus transaction costs. In the idealised world that cost equals the Black–Scholes premium; frictions make it more.
A delta-hedged short option — are you risk-free?
No. You've removed first-order price risk but you're still short Gamma (hurt by big moves) and exposed to Vega (volatility) and Theta. Delta-neutral ≠ risk-free.

Theta (Θ) — time decay

What it is

How much value the option loses purely from one day passing, with everything else held still.

Why it matters

Time is the one risk you cannot hedge — it passes for certain. Long-option holders bleed Theta; option writers earn it. It's the "rent" on optionality.

How it works
\[ \Theta_c=-\frac{S_0 N'(d_1)\sigma}{2\sqrt T}-rKe^{-rT}N(d_2),\qquad \Theta_p=-\frac{S_0 N'(d_1)\sigma}{2\sqrt T}+rKe^{-rT}N(-d_2) \]

\(N'(x)=\frac1{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}\) is the normal density. Θ is usually negative for long options (value erodes), with exceptions (e.g. deep-ITM European puts, high-\(r\) calls). At-the-money near expiry, Θ becomes sharply negative — time value evaporates fastest right before maturity.

Analogy

An option is an ice cube. Even if nothing dramatic happens, it melts a little every day. Near expiry an at-the-money cube melts fastest.

Worked example

Θ = −5 (per year, ÷365 for daily) means the option loses about $5/365 ≈ 1.4 cents of value each day from time alone, before any price move.

Gamma (Γ) — curvature

What it is

How fast Delta itself changes as the underlying moves — the curvature (second derivative) of the price curve.

Why it matters

Gamma is why a delta hedge isn't perfect: high Gamma means Delta drifts quickly, forcing constant rebalancing. Being short Gamma (the option writer) means large moves hurt you in either direction; being long Gamma means big moves help.

How it works
\[ \Gamma=\frac{\partial\Delta}{\partial S}=\frac{\partial^2 c}{\partial S^2}=\frac{N'(d_1)}{S_0\,\sigma\sqrt T}\;\;(>0\text{ always}) \]

Gamma peaks at the money and rises as expiry approaches — exactly where Delta is most unstable.

📈What this shows: Gamma (and the matching call price) vs spot. Gamma is a bell peaked at the strike \(K=50\) — Delta changes fastest there. Cut T toward 0 and the peak grows tall and narrow: near expiry, an at-the-money option's Delta flips violently between 0 and 1.
Analogy

If Delta is your speed, Gamma is how hard you're accelerating. A high-Gamma position is a twitchy steering wheel — tiny moves of the market force big corrections to stay hedged.

Gamma-neutral hedging & the Taylor view

How it works

A small change in the option's value is captured by a second-order Taylor expansion in price and time:

\[ \delta c \approx \Theta\,\delta t + \Delta\,\delta S + \tfrac12\,\Gamma\,(\delta S)^2 \]

Delta-hedging removes the \(\Delta\,\delta S\) term. The leftover \(\tfrac12\Gamma(\delta S)^2\) is the curvature error — exactly the gap a pure delta hedge can't close, and the reason big moves matter even when Δ-neutral.

Making a portfolio Gamma-neutral: a stock/forward has Γ = 0, so you can only change Gamma by adding another option. To kill portfolio Gamma \(\Gamma_\Pi\) using a traded option with gamma \(\Gamma_T\), add

\[ w_T=-\frac{\Gamma_\Pi}{\Gamma_T}\ \text{units, then re-delta-hedge (the new option shifts }\Delta\text{).} \]
Worked example

Portfolio is Δ-neutral with \(\Gamma=-3000\). Traded option has \(\Delta_o=0.62,\ \Gamma_o=1.5\). Add \(3000/1.5=2000\) long options → Γ-neutral. But this adds \(2000\times0.62=1240\) to Delta, so sell 1240 shares to restore Δ-neutrality.

How desks actually run it Delta-hedge daily; monitor Gamma, Vega, Theta and only act on the others when they get too large. Greeks are additive across a book — the portfolio Greek is the weighted sum of the constituents' Greeks, so they net off.
Examiner might ask Gamma & higher-order hedging
Why can't a stock change a portfolio's Gamma?
A stock (or forward) is linear in \(S\): its second derivative is zero. Only instruments with curvature — options — carry Gamma, so only options can adjust it.
You add an option to go Gamma-neutral — what breaks?
Your Delta. The new option has its own Delta, knocking the book off Δ-neutral, so you must re-hedge Delta with the underlying afterward.
Long Gamma vs short Gamma — who likes volatility?
Long Gamma (long options) profits from large moves either way and pays Theta for the privilege. Short Gamma (writer) earns Theta but is hurt by large moves — you're "selling insurance and praying for calm".

Vega, Rho & beyond Black–Scholes

How it works
  • Vega (ν): sensitivity to volatility. Always positive for long options — more shakiness = more chance of a big payoff = more value. (Despite the name, it's not a Greek letter.)
  • Rho (ρ): sensitivity to the interest rate. Calls gain with higher \(r\) (the strike is discounted more), puts lose.

Beyond Black–Scholes, Greeks come from richer models and numerical methods:

  • Models: Heston (stochastic vol → explains the smile), Merton jump-diffusion (sudden jumps → fat tails), binomial/trinomial trees (American/early exercise).
  • Computing Greeks: finite differences (simple, costly), pathwise & likelihood-ratio methods in Monte Carlo, and automatic differentiation (exact, efficient — vital for big books).
Worked example — methods disagree

An American put (\(S_0=K=100,\ r=5\%,\ \sigma=20\%,\ T=1,\ q=2\%\)) priced three ways: Black–Scholes $9.23 (Δ 0.587), Binomial $7.50 (Δ 0.530), Monte Carlo $7.55 (Δ 0.520). They differ because BS ignores early exercise / dividends — a reminder that the model choice is a modelling decision.

Analogy

Vega is sensitivity to how bumpy the road looks ahead; Rho is sensitivity to the cost of the money you borrowed for the trip. The fancier models are better weather forecasts for roads that Black–Scholes assumed were perfectly smooth.

Examiner might ask Vega / Rho / model choice
Sign of Vega for a long call vs long put?
Both positive. Higher volatility widens the distribution of outcomes; since option payoffs are one-sided (capped loss), more dispersion can only help the holder.
Why do calls have positive Rho and puts negative?
A higher rate discounts the strike \(Ke^{-rT}\) more heavily. For a call you pay \(K\) later → cheaper in PV → call worth more. For a put you receive \(K\) later → worth less.
When would you abandon Black–Scholes Greeks?
American/early-exercise features (use trees), pronounced smile/skew (stochastic-vol like Heston), gappy assets (jump-diffusion), or path-dependent exotics (Monte Carlo). The smile itself is the market's signal that constant-σ Greeks are approximate.
One-line story for the exam "The Greeks split an option's risk into pieces: Delta (price slope, the hedge ratio), Gamma (curvature — why the delta hedge needs constant rebalancing), Theta (unavoidable time decay), plus Vega and Rho. Writers harvest the premium by delta-hedging continuously; the leftover \(\tfrac12\Gamma\,\delta S^2\) is what big moves and gamma-hedging are about."
