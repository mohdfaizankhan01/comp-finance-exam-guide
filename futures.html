Futures & Forwards

Contracts to trade at a fixed price on a future date. Unlike options, there's no choice — you're obligated. Their fair price comes from a simple replication argument.

What they are

What it is

A futures (or forward) contract is a binding agreement between two parties to buy/sell an asset at a set price \(K\) on a set future date \(T\). Both sides are obligated — it's not a right.

Why it matters

Futures let farmers lock in a selling price, airlines lock in fuel costs, and investors get exposure to commodities or indices without holding the physical asset. They're a primary hedging and speculation tool, and their pricing is the cleanest example of replication.

How it works

Traded on assets like commodities (metals, grain, energy), equities, currencies and bonds. To stay exposed without taking delivery you "roll over" — close the near contract and open the next. Created originally by/for farmers wanting price certainty on a future harvest.

Analogy

A forward is pre-ordering a product at today's quoted price for delivery next month — but unlike a pre-order you can cancel, here both you and the seller are locked in, win or lose.

Forwards vs futures

How it works
ForwardsFutures
Traded OTC (private)Traded on exchanges (e.g. CME)
Customisable / not standardisedStandardised contracts
Specific delivery dateRange of delivery dates
Settled once, at the endSettled daily (marked to market)
Often physical deliveryUsually closed out before maturity

The big practical difference: futures are exchange-guaranteed and settled daily via margins, removing most counterparty (default) risk; forwards carry that risk privately.

Analogy

A forward is a handshake deal with a neighbour; a future is the same deal run through an escrow agent (the exchange) who checks both wallets every day so neither can walk away.

Margins & daily settlement

What it is

The system the exchange uses to guarantee performance: each side posts collateral (margin), and gains/losses are settled in cash every day.

Why it matters

It's what makes futures safe to trade with strangers — it removes the risk that the loser simply doesn't pay. Understanding margin calls is essential to understanding why futures rarely default.

How it works
  • Initial margin: collateral posted up front.
  • Maintenance margin: a floor; if your balance drops below it, you get a margin call to top back up to the initial level.
  • Excess above the initial margin can be withdrawn.
Worked example

Buy 2 gold futures (100 oz each) at $400. Initial margin $2000/contract ($4000 total), maintenance $1500/contract ($3000 total). Day 1 price → $397: loss \(2\times100\times3=\$600\), balance $3400. After a slide to $393.30, cumulative loss \(-\$1340\), balance $2660 < $3000 → margin call for $1340 to restore $4000.

Analogy

Margin is a poker buy-in that's topped up each hand: the house makes you keep enough chips on the table so you can always cover today's losses, settling up nightly instead of at the end.

Pricing — cost of carry

What it is

The fair futures price is simply today's spot price grown at the risk-free rate to the delivery date — the cost of "carrying" the asset until then.

Why it matters

It's the purest no-arbitrage / replication result in the course: the price is fixed by the cost to build the position, with no probabilities or forecasts involved.

How it works
\[ F_t = S_t\,e^{r(T-t)} \]
  • S_t spot now · r risk-free rate · T−t time to delivery. To provide the asset at \(T\), borrow \(S_t\) now, buy the asset, repay \(S_t e^{r(T-t)}\) at \(T\) — that's the cost, hence the price.
Arbitrage proof Why \(F_t=S_te^{r(T-t)}\)

If \(F_t' > S_te^{r(T-t)}\): borrow cash, buy the asset spot, short the future. At delivery hand over the asset for \(F_t'\), repay \(S_te^{r(T-t)}\), pocket \(F_t'-S_te^{r(T-t)}>0\) risk-free.

If \(F_t'' < S_te^{r(T-t)}\): buy the future, short-sell the asset, invest the proceeds at \(r\). At delivery take delivery for \(F_t''\), having grown the cash to \(S_te^{r(T-t)}\), pocket \(S_te^{r(T-t)}-F_t''>0\).

Both free lunches vanish only at \(F_t=S_te^{r(T-t)}\). \(\;\square\)

Adjust for cash flows on the asset:

\[ \text{income/dividend } I:\quad F_t=(S_t-I)e^{r(T-t)} \qquad\quad \text{yield/cost } q:\quad F_t=S_t e^{(r-q)(T-t)} \]

An income \(I\) (e.g. dividends, coupons) lowers the futures price; a storage cost \(q\) raises it; a convenience yield lowers it.

📈What this shows: futures price vs time to delivery for spot \(S=100\). With net carry \(r-q>0\) the curve rises (contango); make \(q>r\) and it falls (backwardation). In all cases it converges to spot as \(T\to t\).
Analogy

The futures price is "what it costs to keep the asset in storage until handover" — finance cost (interest) pushes it up, any income it throws off (dividends) or a storage bill (commodities) adjusts it.

Worked example (Hull 3.5)

4-month forward on a zero-coupon bond, spot $930, \(r=6\%\). No income, so \(F_0=930\,e^{0.06\cdot4/12}=\mathbf{\$948.79}\) — the delivery price for a contract negotiated today.

Valuing an existing contract

How it works

At inception \(K=F_0\) and the contract is worth \(f=0\). Later, as \(F_t\) moves, its value to the long is the discounted difference between the current futures price and the locked-in delivery price:

\[ f_t=(F_t-K)\,e^{-r(T-t)} \;=\; S_t - K e^{-r(T-t)} \quad(\text{no income}) \]

So holding a future is partly like holding the underlying (\(S_t\) appears directly), plus a rate-dependent term. \(F_t\) itself is set by supply/demand around the carry relationship.

Analogy

Your locked price \(K\) is a coupon. If the market's going rate \(F_t\) drifts above \(K\), your coupon is now valuable; discount that advantage back to today and you have the contract's value.

Worked example

You're long at \(K=948.79\). A month later the futures trades \(F_t=955\), \(r=6\%\), \(T-t=3/12\): \(f=(955-948.79)e^{-0.06\cdot0.25}=6.21\times0.985=\mathbf{\$6.12}\) of value to you.

Contango & backwardation

What it is

Two shapes the futures curve can take: contango = futures above spot (upward-sloping); backwardation = futures below spot (downward-sloping).

Why it matters

The shape reflects market expectations and storage/demand. It also drives the cost of rolling — in contango, rolling forward repeatedly bleeds money, which matters hugely for commodity ETFs.

How it works
  • Contango: high carry/interest, prices expected to rise → \(F>S\).
  • Backwardation: high demand for the asset now, prices expected to fall → \(F<S\).
  • Either way, by convergence \(F_T\to S_T\) at maturity — the contract must settle at spot.
Analogy

Contango is "future concert tickets cost more than today's" (storage/financing premium); backwardation is "everyone wants the asset right now" so spot is bid up above the future.

Examiner might ask Futures probes
How does futures pricing differ from option pricing?
No probabilities, no volatility — pure cost-of-carry replication: \(F=Se^{r(T-t)}\). Options need a model of the price distribution (binomial/Black–Scholes) because the payoff is nonlinear; a future's payoff is linear, so no-arbitrage alone fixes the price.
Why does daily settlement matter?
It limits credit risk: losses are collected daily, so a defaulter can only ever owe one day's move, backed by margin. It's the structural reason futures rarely default versus forwards.
What happens to \(F\) as maturity approaches?
It converges to the spot price \(S_T\) — the carry term \(e^{r(T-t)}\to1\). Contango and backwardation both collapse to spot at delivery.
Why is a future "partly being exposed to the underlying"?
Because its value \(f_t=S_t-Ke^{-r(T-t)}\) moves one-for-one with \(S_t\). You also pick up sensitivity to \(r\), but the dominant driver is the spot.
One-line story for the exam "A future is an obligation to trade at a fixed price later. Its fair price is pure cost-of-carry, \(F=Se^{r(T-t)}\), forced by no-arbitrage replication — no probabilities needed. Exchanges remove default risk via daily margining, and the curve sits in contango or backwardation but always converges to spot at delivery."
