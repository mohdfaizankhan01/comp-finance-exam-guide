Simple returns

What it is

A return is just the percentage change in price between two dates — how much you made or lost as a fraction of what you put in.

Why it matters

Prices alone are meaningless for comparison — a CHF 5 gain on a CHF 10 stock (+50%) is wildly better than CHF 5 on a CHF 1000 stock (+0.5%). Returns put every investment on the same scale so we can compare, add up, and model them. Almost everything later (volatility, Sharpe ratios, portfolio theory) is built on returns, not prices.

How it works

Let \(P(t)\) be the price at time \(t\). The simple (or "arithmetic") return over one period is:

\[ R_1(t) = \frac{P(t)-P(t-1)}{P(t-1)} = \frac{P(t)}{P(t-1)} - 1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad 1+R_1(t)=\frac{P(t)}{P(t-1)} \]
  • P(t) price now · P(t-1) price one period ago
  • R₁(t) the one-period simple return (a fraction; ×100 for %)
  • The quantity \(1+R\) is the gross return — the growth multiplier.

Notice the asymmetry: a price going 50→60 is +20%, but 60→50 is only −16.7%. Equal franc moves give unequal percentage moves, because the denominator changes.

Analogy

A return is a report card grade as a percentage, not raw points. Scoring 5 extra points means nothing until you know the test was out of 10 (huge) or out of 1000 (trivial). The denominator is everything.

Worked example

\(P(t-1)=50,\ P(t)=60\):   \(R = 60/50 - 1 = 1.20 - 1 = 0.20 = \mathbf{+20\%}\).
Reverse it, \(60\to 50\):   \(R = 50/60 - 1 = 0.8\overline{3} - 1 = \mathbf{-16.7\%}\).

Examiner might ask Why do equal-size price moves give unequal returns?
Why is +20% then −16.7% not a wash?
Because the percentages are taken against different bases. +20% is measured on 50, −16.7% on 60. To return to the start you need a smaller percentage drop than the percentage gain that took you up. This is exactly why a stock that falls 50% must rise 100% to recover.
So how should I combine returns to avoid this trap?
Work with gross returns \((1+R)\) and multiply them, or switch to log returns which add (see below). Never simply sum simple returns over time.
Don't say this "A 20% gain followed by a 20% loss puts me back where I started." It doesn't — \(1.20\times0.80 = 0.96\), a 4% loss. Saying this signals you don't understand compounding.

Multi-period returns — why returns multiply

What it is

The total return over several periods is found by multiplying the gross returns of each period together, not by adding the returns.

Why it matters

This is compounding — the engine of all long-term investing. Getting it wrong (summing instead of compounding) is the single most common beginner error, and an examiner will test whether you know the difference.

How it works

Over two periods the prices telescope:

\[ 1+R_2(t)=\frac{P(t)}{P(t-2)}=\underbrace{\frac{P(t)}{P(t-1)}}_{1+R_1(t)}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{P(t-1)}{P(t-2)}}_{1+R_1(t-1)} \]

Generalising to \(n\) periods gives a product:

\[ 1+R_n(t)=\prod_{i=0}^{n-1}\big(1+R_1(t-i)\big) \]

The big \(\prod\) means "multiply all of these together". Each factor is one period's growth multiplier.

Analogy

Like resizing a photo repeatedly. Enlarge by 20%, then shrink by 10% — you don't do "20−10". You scale by 1.20, then by 0.90. Each step works on the already-resized picture, so the operations multiply.

Worked example

Prices 10 → 12 → 14 → 16. Period returns: +20%, +16.7%, +14.3%.

Wrong (sum): \(20\%+16.7\%+14.3\% = 50.9\%\) ✗
Right (product): \((1.20)(1.167)(1.143)-1 = 1.60-1 = \mathbf{60\%}\) ✓
Sanity check directly: \(16/10 - 1 = 60\%\). The product matches; the sum does not.

Examiner might ask When is summing returns ever acceptable?
Can I ever just add returns?
Yes — if you use log (continuous) returns, they add exactly over time. And for very small returns over short horizons, simple returns are approximately additive because \(\ln(1+R)\approx R\). But the exact rule for simple returns is always multiplication.
Why does the textbook bother to point this out?
Because performance reporting bugs and naïve "average return" claims almost always come from summing or arithmetic-averaging when one should compound (geometric mean).

Portfolio returns

What it is

The return of a basket of assets is the weighted average of the individual asset returns, using the money allocated to each as the weights.

Why it matters

It tells you that across assets at one moment, returns combine linearly (a weighted sum) — in contrast to across time, where they compound. Knowing which combination rule applies in which direction is the key insight, and it underpins Portfolio Theory.

How it works

Weights \(\alpha_i\) are the fractions of wealth in each asset, with \(\sum_{i=1}^n\alpha_i=1\). Define the portfolio return \(R_p=\sum_i\alpha_i R_i\). Check it reproduces total wealth growth:

\[ \sum_i \alpha_i(1+R_i)=\underbrace{\sum_i\alpha_i}_{=1}+\sum_i\alpha_i R_i = 1 + R_p \]

So the portfolio's growth multiplier is the weighted average of the assets' multipliers — exactly what "putting 60% here, 40% there" should mean.

Analogy

Like a blended exam mark where coursework is 30% and the final is 70%: your overall mark is \(0.3\times\text{coursework}+0.7\times\text{final}\). The weights are how much each counts.

Worked example

60% in asset A (returns +10%), 40% in asset B (returns −5%):
\(R_p = 0.6(0.10)+0.4(-0.05)=0.06-0.02=\mathbf{+4\%}\).

Examiner might ask Returns add across assets but multiply across time — explain.
Why two different rules?
Across assets at the same instant, you split one pot of money — the wealth-weighted average is linear. Across time, each period's gain becomes next period's base, so multipliers stack multiplicatively. Different directions, different algebra.
Does the weighted-average rule hold for log returns of a portfolio too?
No — that's a subtle trap. \(r_p=\ln(1+\sum\alpha_iR_i)\neq\sum\alpha_i r_i\). Log returns add neatly over time, but the portfolio log return is not the weighted average of asset log returns. Use simple returns for cross-sectional (portfolio) aggregation.

Annualisation

What it is

Rescaling a return measured over one horizon (a day, a month) into the equivalent return per year, so different investments can be compared on common ground.

Why it matters

"+1% this month" and "+0.04% today" are incomparable until annualised. Because returns compound, you annualise by raising to a power, never by multiplying.

How it works
\[ 1+R_a=(1+R_m)^{12},\qquad 1+R_a=(1+R_d)^{256} \]

R_a annual · R_m monthly · R_d daily. We use 12 months, and ~256 trading days (markets aren't open 365 days; 250 and 252 are also common conventions).

Note / convention The slides use 256 trading days (it's a tidy \(16^2\), handy because volatility then scales by \(\sqrt{256}=16\)). Many texts use 250 or 252. State your assumption in the exam — examiners care that you declare the convention, not which one you pick.
Analogy

Like converting a per-night hotel rate to an annual cost while it keeps reinvesting — you don't multiply the nightly rate by 365 if interest accrues on interest; you compound it.

Worked example

A 1% monthly return annualises to \((1.01)^{12}-1 = 1.1268-1 = \mathbf{12.68\%}\), not 12%. The extra 0.68% is compounding.

Don't say this "1% per month is 12% per year." That undercounts compounding. Worse: "to annualise I multiply daily return by 256."

Time value of money

What it is

A franc today is worth more than a franc tomorrow, because today's franc can be invested to earn interest. "Discounting" reverses this to value future cash flows today.

Why it matters

It is the foundation under bond pricing, option pricing and every valuation in finance. Whenever cash arrives at different times, you cannot add it up until you bring it to a common date.

How it works

With return \(R\) per period, present value \(PV\) grows to a future value:

\[ FV_n = PV\,(1+R)^n \]

If interest is paid \(m\) times per period, slice the rate and multiply the periods:

\[ FV_n^{m}=PV\Big(1+\tfrac{R}{m}\Big)^{m\,n} \]

Letting payments become infinitely frequent (\(m\to\infty\)) gives continuous compounding:

\[ FV_n^{\infty}=PV\lim_{m\to\infty}\Big(1+\tfrac{R}{m}\Big)^{mn}=PV\,e^{Rn} \]
Derivation Why the limit is \(e^{Rn}\)

Set \(k=m/R\), so \(m=kR\) and as \(m\to\infty\), \(k\to\infty\):

\[ \Big(1+\tfrac{R}{m}\Big)^{mn}=\Big(1+\tfrac1k\Big)^{kRn}=\left[\Big(1+\tfrac1k\Big)^{k}\right]^{Rn}. \]

The bracket is the textbook definition of Euler's number: \(\big(1+\tfrac1k\big)^{k}\to e\). Raising the limit to the power \(Rn\) gives \(e^{Rn}\). \(\;\square\)

Analogy

A promise of cake next year is worth less than cake now — you could have eaten (or sold) today's cake and put the proceeds to work. Discounting asks: how much cake-now equals that future promise?

Worked example

\(PV=1000\), \(R=10\%\), \(n=1\) year. Compounding more often gives slightly more:

FrequencyFormulaValue
Annual1000·(1.10)¹1100.0
Quarterly1000·(1+0.1/4)⁴1103.8
Weekly1000·(1+0.1/52)⁵²1105.1
Daily1000·(1+0.1/365)³⁶⁵1105.2
Continuous1000·e^0.11105.2

More frequent compounding helps, but with rapidly diminishing returns — it converges to the continuous ceiling \(e^{0.1}\).

📈What this shows: CHF 1000 at 10% for one year, as compounding gets more frequent. It climbs fast then flattens to the continuous limit (dashed) — extra frequency buys less and less.

Continuous (log) returns

What it is

The log return is the natural logarithm of the gross return — the return you'd quote if interest compounded continuously.

Why it matters

Two superpowers: log returns add across time (no messy products), and they are symmetric (a move up and the equal move back down cancel exactly). That is why quants model log returns — it makes the calculus of Black–Scholes and time-series models clean.

How it works
\[ r(t)=\ln\big(1+R(t)\big)=\ln\frac{P_t}{P_{t-1}}=\ln P_t-\ln P_{t-1} \]

Over two periods they simply add:

\[ r_2(t)=\ln\frac{P_t}{P_{t-2}}=\ln\frac{P_t}{P_{t-1}}+\ln\frac{P_{t-1}}{P_{t-2}}=r_1(t)+r_1(t-1),\qquad r_n(t)=\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}r_1(t-i) \]

Link to the continuous rate: since \(PV(1+R)^n=PV\,e^{rn}\), we get \(r=\ln(1+R)\), and because \(\ln(1+R)\lt R\) for \(R\gt 0\), the continuous rate is always slightly below the simple rate.

  • Example: \(R=10\%\Rightarrow r=\ln(1.1)=9.53\%\), and indeed \(1000\,e^{0.0953}=1100\). ✓
Analogy

Log returns are like measuring distance in "doublings" (octaves) instead of metres. On a ratio scale, going up then back down by the same factor returns you exactly to the start, and multi-leg journeys just add up.

Worked example

50→60: \(r=\ln(60/50)=\ln 1.2=+18.23\%\). 60→50: \(r=\ln(50/60)=-18.23\%\). Perfectly symmetric — the +20% / −16.7% asymmetry of simple returns has vanished.

📈What this shows: simple vs log return for the same price ratio. They agree for small moves (the dashed 45° line) but diverge for large ones — log returns are symmetric about zero, simple returns are not.
Examiner might ask Trade-offs between simple and log returns
When do you use each?
Log returns: aggregating over time, statistical/stochastic modelling, anything involving calculus. Simple returns: aggregating across assets in a portfolio (weighted average), and when reporting actual P&L to a client.
Why is \(r\lt R\)?
\(\ln(1+R)\) is concave and lies below the line \(y=R\) for \(R\gt 0\). Intuitively, continuous compounding earns interest-on-interest constantly, so a smaller continuous rate achieves the same end value as a given simple rate.
Are they ever equal?
Only in the limit \(R\to0\), where \(\ln(1+R)\approx R\). For small daily moves the two are nearly identical, which is why the approximation is so widely used.

Bookkeeping, currency risk & hedging

What it is

Tracking a portfolio's Net Asset Value (NAV) in your home currency, which means foreign holdings carry two risks: the asset itself, and the exchange rate.

Why it matters

A Swiss investor in Apple can be right about Apple and still lose money if the dollar falls. Recognising and optionally removing (hedging) that FX risk is a core practical skill — and a natural bridge to options, futures and forwards.

How it works

NAV in CHF for a USD asset:

\[ \text{NAV} = (\text{shares}\times P_{\text{asset,USD}})\times (\text{USD\_CHF}) + \text{Cash} \]

If USD_CHF moves against you, the asset can recover fully in USD yet your CHF NAV still ends lower. A hedge (e.g. a short USD position via a forward) cancels the FX leg — but it has a cost (the interest-rate differential, the "carry").

Analogy

Buying a foreign stock is like betting on a horse in a foreign country: you can pick the winning horse (the asset) and still come home poorer if that country's currency tanks before you cash out. Hedging is buying travel-money insurance on the exchange rate — useful, but it isn't free.

Worked example

Buy 10 AAPL at $45 with USD_CHF = 0.90. Apple ends back at $49 (up in USD) but USD_CHF slips 0.90→0.88. The equity leg is positive, the FX leg negative; net CHF P&L can be slightly negative (≈ −CHF 0.8 in the slide). Add a hedge and the NAV becomes far less volatile — only the equity risk remains — at a small hedging cost (≈ CHF 2.4).

Examiner might ask Currency risk & hedging
Name the two risks a CHF investor faces buying a US stock.
Equity (price) risk and FX (USD_CHF) risk. NAV depends on the product of the two, plus any cash/carry.
Can FX risk be removed, and what's the catch?
Yes — short the foreign currency (typically via a forward/future) sized to the position. The catch is cost: you give up (or pay) the interest-rate differential, called the carry, and you must rebalance the hedge as the position value moves.
Does hedging make you richer?
No — it reduces variance, not expected return (often it slightly lowers return via cost). You hedge to control risk, not to chase return.
One-line story for the exam "Returns turn prices into comparable percentages; they compound (multiply) over time but weight-average across assets; log returns make time-aggregation additive and symmetric; and because money has a time value, future cash must be discounted — continuous compounding being the \(e^{rt}\) limit of ever-more-frequent interest."
