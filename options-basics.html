What an option is

What it is

An option is the right, but not the obligation, to buy (a call) or sell (a put) an asset at a fixed price (the strike \(K\)) up to a fixed date (the maturity \(T\)).

Why it matters

Options are insurance contracts on price. They let you cap your downside, take leveraged bets, or sell protection for income. The whole field of derivatives pricing exists to answer: what is the fair premium for that right?

How it works

Four basic positions arise from {buy, sell} × {call, put}:

PositionYou holdPay or receive premium?Your view
Long callright to buypaybullish
Long putright to sellpaybearish / protection
Short call (write)obligation to sell if exercisedreceiveneutral/bearish, risky
Short put (write)obligation to buy if exercisedreceiveneutral/bullish

European options can be exercised only at \(T\); American options at any time up to \(T\). Most traded options are American, but theory usually starts with European for simplicity.

Analogy

A call is a "hold this price for me" coupon. You pay a small fee to lock in buying a concert ticket at face value next month: if the price soars, you cash in the coupon; if it crashes, you bin the coupon and buy cheaper. You only ever lose the fee. The seller of the coupon pockets your fee but is on the hook if the price soars.

Worked example

Long call, premium \(c=\$5\), strike \(K=\$60\). At maturity if the stock \(S_T=\$65\): exercise — buy at 60, worth 65 → payoff \(\$5\), profit \(5-5=\$0\) (break-even). If \(S_T=\$75\): payoff \(\$15\), profit \(\$10\). If \(S_T=\$50\): don't exercise, lose only the \(\$5\) premium.

Payoffs & profit diagrams

What it is

A diagram of your profit at maturity as a function of the underlying price \(S_T\). It's the single most useful picture in options.

Why it matters

It shows at a glance your maximum loss, maximum gain and break-even. It also reveals the dangerous asymmetry of writing options: a short call has theoretically unlimited loss.

How it works

Profit = payoff − premium (signs flip for short positions):

\[ \text{Long call: } \max(S_T-K,0)-c \qquad \text{Long put: } \max(K-S_T,0)-p \]
\[ \text{Short call: } c-\max(S_T-K,0) \qquad \text{Short put: } p-\max(K-S_T,0) \]

A long position is the mirror image of the matching short across the horizontal axis — what one side gains, the other loses (options are zero-sum before fees).

📈What this shows: the four basic positions. Long call/put have capped loss (the premium) and large upside. Short call (red dashed) is the scary one — losses grow without limit as the price rises. Drag the sliders to see how strike and premium shift break-even.
Analogy

Buying an option is like buying insurance: small fixed premium, big payout in the bad scenario. Writing an option is like being the insurance company: steady premium income, but you carry the tail risk — and for a short call that tail is bottomless.

Don't say this "Writing options is safe because you collect the premium." A naked short call has unlimited downside. Always mention the asymmetric risk of writing.

Variables & moneyness

What it is

The handful of inputs that set an option's price, and the jargon for where the strike sits relative to the spot.

Why it matters

Every Greek (next lecture) is a derivative of the price with respect to one of these. Knowing the six inputs cold is the foundation for everything that follows.

How it works
  • S₀ spot price now · K strike · T time to maturity
  • σ volatility of the underlying · r risk-free rate · payments (dividends/coupons)

Moneyness (for a call): \(K=S_0\) at the money; \(K<S_0\) in the money (immediate exercise value); \(K>S_0\) out of the money.

Note / careful The slide states call moneyness as "\(K>S_0\) in the money". That's reversed. A call is in-the-money when \(S_0>K\) (i.e. \(K<S_0\)) — you'd want to exercise. For a put it's the opposite: in-the-money when \(K>S_0\). Use intrinsic value to stay safe: a call's intrinsic value is \(\max(S_0-K,0)\); positive ⇒ in the money.
Analogy

Moneyness is "how good is my coupon right now?" In-the-money = the coupon already beats the market price. Out-of-the-money = the coupon is useless at today's price but still has hope while time remains.

Worked example

Spot \(S_0=\$52\). A \(\$50\) call is in-the-money (intrinsic \(\$2\)). A \(\$55\) call is out-of-the-money (intrinsic \(\$0\), but time value > 0). A \(\$55\) put is in-the-money (intrinsic \(\$3\)).

No-arbitrage price bounds

What it is

Hard limits on what a call or put can cost, derived purely from "there should be no free lunch" — before any pricing model.

Why it matters

They show the power of arbitrage arguments: you can pin prices without assuming anything about probabilities. This reasoning is the backbone of all derivative pricing, and examiners love it.

How it works
\[ S_0 - K e^{-rT} \;\le\; c \;\le\; S_0 \qquad\qquad K e^{-rT} - S_0 \;\le\; p \;\le\; K \]
  • c, p call / put price · Ke^{-rT} present value of the strike (discounting the strike back to today).
Derivation Upper bound \(c\le S_0\) and lower bound \(c\ge S_0-Ke^{-rT}\)

Upper bound. If \(c>S_0\), sell the call and buy the stock. You pocket \(c-S_0>0\) today and own the stock, which always covers your obligation to deliver. Risk-free profit ⇒ impossible. Hence \(c\le S_0\).

Lower bound (portfolio comparison). Compare two portfolios:

  • A: one call + cash \(Ke^{-rT}\) (which grows to \(K\) at \(T\)).
  • B: one share \(S\).

At maturity: if \(S_T>K\), A = \((S_T-K)+K = S_T\); if \(S_T\le K\), A = \(0+K=K>S_T\). So A is always ≥ B. By no-arbitrage today's values obey the same order: \(c+Ke^{-rT}\ge S_0\), i.e. \(c\ge S_0-Ke^{-rT}\). A symmetric argument gives \(p\ge Ke^{-rT}-S_0\). \(\;\square\)

Analogy

An option can never be worth more than the thing it controls (a coupon to buy a car can't cost more than the car), and never so cheap that owning "call + cash" beats owning the stock outright for free.

Worked example

\(S_0=\$50\), \(K=\$48\), \(r=5\%\), \(T=1\): \(Ke^{-rT}=48e^{-0.05}=\$45.66\). So \(c\ge 50-45.66=\$4.34\) and \(c\le \$50\). Any quoted call below $4.34 is an arbitrage.

Put–call parity

What it is

An exact equation linking the price of a European call and put with the same strike and maturity. Know three of the four prices and you know the fourth.

Why it matters

It means calls and puts are not independent instruments — they are two views of the same thing. It's a model-free relationship (no Black–Scholes needed), used constantly to spot mispricings and to synthesise one option from the other.

How it works
\[ c + Ke^{-rT} \;=\; p + S_0 \]
Derivation Two portfolios worth the same at \(T\)

A: call + cash \(Ke^{-rT}\).   C: put + one share.

At maturity both are worth \(\max(S_T,K)\):

  • If \(S_T>K\): A = \((S_T-K)+K=S_T\); C = \(0+S_T=S_T\). ✓
  • If \(S_T\le K\): A = \(0+K=K\); C = \((K-S_T)+S_T=K\). ✓

Equal payoff in every state ⇒ equal price today: \(c+Ke^{-rT}=p+S_0\). \(\;\square\)

Rearranged, it shows a long call + short put (same K, T) replicates a forward on the stock: \(c-p=S_0-Ke^{-rT}\).

Analogy

Two recipes, same cake. "Call + cash" and "put + stock" are different ingredient lists that bake into identical outcomes — so they must cost the same, or you'd buy the cheap recipe and sell the dear one.

Worked example

\(S_0=\$50\), \(K=\$50\), \(r=5\%\), \(T=1\), call \(c=\$6\). Then \(p=c+Ke^{-rT}-S_0=6+50e^{-0.05}-50=6+47.56-50=\$3.56\). If the market quotes the put at $5, sell the put + stock, buy call + bond, lock in the gap.

Examiner might ask Put–call parity probes
Does parity hold for American options?
Not as an equality — early exercise breaks the replication. For American options you get inequalities (bounds) instead of the clean equation. The clean parity is a European result.
What assumptions does it need?
No arbitrage, ability to borrow/lend at \(r\), no dividends (or include their PV: \(c+Ke^{-rT}+D=p+S_0\)), no transaction costs. Notably it needs no assumption about volatility or the price distribution.
If implied volatility differs between the call and put at the same strike, what's wrong?
Nothing fundamental — parity is about prices, not vols. If both are priced consistently, the implied vols of a European call and put at the same K,T are equal; a persistent gap signals dividends, rates, or a quoting/arbitrage issue.

Trading strategies

What it is

Combining options (and the stock) to sculpt a desired payoff shape — capping risk, cheapening a bet, or profiting from a view on volatility.

Why it matters

It shows options are building blocks. Protective puts let pension funds floor their losses; spreads let you express precise views cheaply.

How it works
  • Protective put = long stock + long put → a call-like shape with a floor; this is exactly put–call parity in action.
  • Bull spread = long call at \(K_1\) + short call at \(K_2\) (\(K_2>K_1\)) → bullish, but cheaper and with capped upside.
  • Butterfly = long call \(K_1\) + 2 short calls \(K_2\) + long call \(K_3\) → bets the price lands near \(K_2\) (low volatility).
📈What this shows: a protective put (stock + put). Below the strike your losses are floored; above it you keep the upside minus the premium — the classic insurance shape, and a visual proof of put–call parity.
Analogy

A protective put is collision insurance on your car: keep all the upside if the car holds its value, but the insurer caps how much you can lose in a crash.

Pension-fund use A fund exposed to equities buys a basket of puts to floor drawdowns. The cost is the premiums, so the job is to find the sweet spot between protection and the drag on returns.

Binomial tree pricing

What it is

Price an option by chopping time into steps where the price can only go up or down, building a fully-hedged portfolio at each node, and working backwards from maturity.

Why it matters

It is the most intuitive pricing method and the cleanest demonstration of the core idea: if you can hedge away all the risk, the option's price can't depend on the probability of going up — only on the no-arbitrage replication cost. It also handles American and exotic options where formulas fail.

How it works

Over one step the stock goes to \(S_0u\) or \(S_0d\) (with \(u=1/d\)); the option becomes \(f_u\) or \(f_d\). Build a portfolio: short the option, long \(\Delta\) shares. Choose \(\Delta\) so the portfolio is worth the same up or down — risk-free:

\[ S_0u\,\Delta - f_u = S_0d\,\Delta - f_d \;\Rightarrow\; \Delta=\frac{f_u-f_d}{S_0u-S_0d} \]

A risk-free portfolio must earn \(r\), which after algebra gives the price as a discounted expectation under the risk-neutral probability \(p\):

\[ f=e^{-rT}\big(p\,f_u+(1-p)\,f_d\big),\qquad p=\frac{e^{rT}-d}{u-d} \]

With volatility \(\sigma\) over step length \(T\), the standard calibration is \(u=e^{\sigma\sqrt{T}},\ d=e^{-\sigma\sqrt{T}}\).

Derivation From the hedged portfolio to the formula for \(f\)

Today the portfolio costs \(P=S_0\Delta - f\). Being risk-free it must equal the discounted certain value \(P=(S_0u\Delta-f_u)e^{-rT}\). Equate and substitute \(\Delta=\frac{f_u-f_d}{S_0(u-d)}\):

\[ f=S_0\Delta-(S_0u\Delta-f_u)e^{-rT}. \]

Collecting terms and writing \(p=\dfrac{e^{rT}-d}{u-d}\) yields \(f=e^{-rT}\!\big(pf_u+(1-p)f_d\big)\). Note \(p\) looks like a probability but is the risk-neutral one — it makes the stock grow at \(r\): \(e^{rT}=pu+(1-p)d\). \(\;\square\)

Analogy

Like pricing a bet by building the opposite bet that exactly cancels it. Once you can perfectly offset every outcome, your luck doesn't matter — the fair price is just what it costs to assemble the offset, grown at the risk-free rate.

Worked example

European call, \(K=\$21\), \(T=3\) months, \(S_0=\$20\), \(u=1.1\), \(d=0.909\), \(r=12\%\). Up: \(S=22\Rightarrow f_u=22-21=1\). Down: \(S=18.2\Rightarrow f_d=0\).

\(p=\dfrac{e^{0.12\cdot0.25}-0.909}{1.1-0.909}=\dfrac{1.0305-0.909}{0.191}=0.6357\).
\(f=e^{-0.12\cdot0.25}(0.6357\cdot1+0.3643\cdot0)=\mathbf{\$0.62}\).
Hedge ratio \(\Delta=\dfrac{1-0}{22-18.2}=\mathbf{0.262}\) shares per option written.

Examiner might ask Binomial-tree probes
Why doesn't the real probability of an up-move appear in the price?
Because we hedged. A delta-hedged portfolio has the same value in both states, so it's riskless and must earn \(r\). The price then depends only on \(u,d,r\) — not on how likely "up" really is. This is the heart of risk-neutral pricing.
What is \(p\), really?
The risk-neutral (not real-world) probability: the value that makes the stock's expected growth equal the risk-free rate. It's a pricing device, not a forecast.
Why use many steps, and what's the cost?
One step is too coarse. More steps converge to the true (Black–Scholes) price, but \(n\) steps imply up to \(2^n\) paths — 30 steps is a billion — so it gets expensive in time and memory. Recombining trees (\(u=1/d\)) tame this to \(O(n^2)\) nodes.

Black–Scholes

What it is

A closed-form formula for the price of a European call or put, obtained by taking the binomial idea to a continuous-time limit where the stock follows a random walk (geometric Brownian motion).

Why it matters

It turned option pricing from art into engineering (Nobel Prize, 1997) and gives instant prices and Greeks for vanilla options. Even where it's wrong (the volatility smile), it's the universal language — quotes are made in "implied vol".

How it works
\[ c = S_0\,N(d_1) - Ke^{-rT}N(d_2),\qquad p = Ke^{-rT}N(-d_2) - S_0 N(-d_1) \]
\[ d_1=\frac{\ln(S_0/K)+\big(r+\tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\big)T}{\sigma\sqrt T},\qquad d_2=d_1-\sigma\sqrt T \]
  • N(x) standard normal CDF — probability a standard normal is below \(x\).
  • N(d₂) ≈ risk-neutral probability the call finishes in the money; N(d₁) is the call's Delta (hedge ratio).
  • Inputs are exactly the six variables from earlier: \(S_0,K,T,\sigma,r\) (no dividends here).
Assumptions (say these in the exam) constant volatility \(\sigma\) and rate \(r\); log-normal prices / continuous paths (no jumps); frictionless markets (no costs, continuous hedging); European exercise. Each failed assumption is a doorway to a better model (Heston for stochastic vol, Merton for jumps, trees for early exercise).
📈What this shows: the Black–Scholes call price vs spot (purple), with strike \(K=\$50\), \(r=5\%\). It sits above the kinked payoff at expiry (grey) — the gap is time value. Raise σ or T and the smooth curve lifts; as \(T\to0\) it collapses onto the payoff. The dashed line is the lower bound \(S_0-Ke^{-rT}\).
Analogy

Black–Scholes is the continuous-shutter-speed photo of the binomial tree's flip-book. Same scene (hedge away risk, discount at \(r\)), but instead of discrete up/down jumps the price drifts and shakes continuously, and the two probabilities \(N(d_1),N(d_2)\) replace the tree's \(p\).

Worked example

The binomial example above (\(S_0=20,K=21,...\)) priced the call at $0.62 with one crude step. Black–Scholes is the limit the tree converges to as you add steps — they meet. Use the chart: at \(S_0=K=50\), an ATM call with σ=20%, T=0.5 is worth a few dollars of pure time value.

Sanity check Black–Scholes respects the bounds

High vol: as \(\sigma\to\infty\), \(d_1\to+\infty\Rightarrow N(d_1)\to1\) and \(d_2\to-\infty\Rightarrow N(d_2)\to0\), so \(c\to S_0\) — the upper bound.

Note / correction The slide writes the low-vol limit as "\(\sigma\to0,\ d_1\to\infty,\ N(d_1)\to1,\ d_2\to\infty,\ N(d_2)\to1\Rightarrow c\ge S_0-Ke^{-rT}\)". That sign reasoning only holds when the call is in the money (\(S_0>Ke^{-rT}\)). Correctly, as \(\sigma\to0\) the option loses all time value and \(c\to\max(S_0-Ke^{-rT},0)\): the intrinsic value. In the money it equals the lower bound; out of the money it goes to 0.

The volatility smile

What it is

Implied volatility is the σ you must plug into Black–Scholes to match the market price. Plotted against strike, it bends into a "smile" instead of being flat.

Why it matters

The smile is the market telling you Black–Scholes is only a model. If BS were exactly right, implied vol would be constant across all strikes and maturities. It isn't — so deep out-of-the-money options (crash protection) are priced richer, reflecting fat tails.

How it works

Real return distributions have fatter tails than the normal (and are skewed, especially in equities). Extreme moves are more likely than BS assumes, so the market pays up for far-from-the-money options — pushing their implied vol above the at-the-money level. The result is a smile (or a skew/smirk in equities, where downside puts are dearest).

📈What this shows: a stylised implied-vol smile. Black–Scholes predicts the flat dashed line; the market trades the curved solid line, with richer vols in the wings — evidence of fat tails and the model's limits.
Analogy

BS assumes the road is uniformly bumpy. The smile is drivers admitting the edges of the road (extreme prices) are bumpier than the middle — so they pay more to insure against ending up out there.

Examiner might ask Smile / implied vol
What is implied volatility?
The single volatility input that makes the Black–Scholes price equal the observed market price — found by inverting the formula numerically. It's the market's expectation of future vol, embedded in the option price.
If BS were correct, what would the smile look like?
A flat horizontal line — implied vol independent of strike and maturity. The observed curvature is direct evidence that the constant-vol, log-normal assumption is violated.
Which models fix it?
Stochastic-volatility models (Heston) let σ itself vary randomly; jump-diffusion (Merton) adds sudden jumps producing fat tails. Both generate smiles/skews endogenously.
One-line story for the exam "An option is the right (not duty) to trade at a fixed price; its payoff is a hockey-stick, its price is bounded by no-arbitrage, and calls and puts are tied by put–call parity. We price it by hedging away the risk — discretely with a binomial tree, or in continuous time with Black–Scholes — and the market's volatility smile reminds us BS is only a model."
