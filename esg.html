ESG / Sustainable Investing

Folding environmental, social and governance factors into investment decisions — measured by imperfect scores, acted on through several strategies.

Why sustainable investing

What it is

Investing that weighs a company's Environmental, Social and Governance behaviour alongside its financials.

Why it matters

Climate change, biodiversity loss, social inequality and governance failures are real risks and real externalities. Finance can't solve them alone, but it can steer capital. It's a fast-growing field with rising regulation — and a likely exam topic precisely because it's current and nuanced.

Analogy

Like nutrition labels for companies: traditional analysis tells you the price; ESG tells you what's in the product (carbon, labour practices, board quality) so you can avoid choices that look cheap but are harmful.

Definition, pillars & themes

How it works
  • E — Environmental: carbon emissions, resource use, pollution, climate risk.
  • S — Social: labour practices, human rights, gender equality, community impact.
  • G — Governance: board structure, executive pay, shareholder rights, transparency.

Sustainable investing is daunting: data is patchy, definitions vary, and it's easy to greenwash. Related acronyms: SDG (UN Sustainable Development Goals), ITR (Implied Temperature Rise), carbon Scopes 1/2/3 (direct, energy, supply-chain emissions).

Analogy

E, S and G are three separate report-card subjects. A company can ace governance but flunk emissions — and a single overall grade can hide that.

Scoring

How it works

Providers (MSCI, Sustainalytics, …) issue ESG scores — typically a weighted average of E, S and G pillar scores, mapped to a ranking. Key points:

  • Many systems disagree, but their scores are highly correlated overall.
  • Corporates are scored more than governments (data on states is improving).
  • Carbon emissions are measured/estimated across Scopes 1-3; the future is modelled via scenarios (e.g. Implied Temperature Rise).
  • Scores tend to rise over time (companies improve disclosure/behaviour — Lundström 2021).
  • Credit ratings do not include ESG — they're separate lenses.
Analogy

ESG scores are like different restaurant-review sites: they rarely give identical stars, but a place everyone rates highly is probably genuinely good. Disagreement at the margins is normal.

Key nuance to mention ESG data is noisy and providers disagree (low "rating consensus" at the company level even if broadly correlated). Always flag data quality and comparability as the central challenge — it signals real understanding.

Strategies

How it works
  • Integration: bake ESG metrics into normal analysis/benchmarks (dashboards, climate scores).
  • Exclusion: screen out the worst (coal, controversial weapons, norm violators).
  • Engagement: dialogue with issuers to push change (e.g. on child labour in cocoa supply chains).
  • Voting: exercise shareholder voting rights at AGMs (e.g. against abusive executive pay).
  • Impact: deliberately fund measurable positive outcomes.
Analogy

As a shareholder you can refuse to shop somewhere (exclusion), complain to management (engagement), vote at the AGM (voting), or seek out the good guys (impact) — different levers, different effort/effect.

In practice (compenswiss example)

How it works

A real pension fund (compenswiss) combines four pillars — integration, voting (~100 AGMs/year in CH), engagement (dialogue with 150+ issuers, e.g. cocoa child labour, via the cofounded ASIR), and exclusions (~100 companies, normative + coal). Carbon-footprint analysis of the portfolio (tCO₂e / $M invested) tracks progress vs benchmark over time.

The recurring theme: data and tools are essential — you can't manage what you can't measure, and traversing data through time and structures is what reveals reality (and unexpected consequences).

Analogy

Like a household trying to cut its carbon footprint: you need a meter (data) to know where the energy goes, then a mix of switching suppliers (exclusion), nagging the landlord (engagement) and voting in the building committee (voting).

Examiner might ask ESG probes
What do E, S and G stand for, with an example each?
Environmental (carbon emissions), Social (labour/human rights, gender equality), Governance (board structure, executive pay). An overall ESG score is usually a weighted average of the three pillars.
Biggest challenge in ESG investing?
Data — it's patchy, inconsistent across providers, and prone to greenwashing. Scores are broadly correlated but disagree at the company level; governments are under-covered.
Name the main ESG strategies.
Integration, exclusion, engagement, voting, and impact — from passively factoring ESG in, to actively screening out, pressuring, voting, or directly funding good outcomes.
Do ESG factors appear in credit ratings?
No — traditional credit ratings (S&P, Moody's) assess default risk only and don't incorporate sustainability; ESG is a separate, parallel assessment.
One-line story for the exam "ESG investing weighs Environmental, Social and Governance behaviour beside financials. It's measured by provider scores (broadly correlated but noisy — data is the key challenge) and acted on via integration, exclusion, engagement, voting and impact. Finance can't fix sustainability alone, but data-driven tools let it measure progress and steer capital."
