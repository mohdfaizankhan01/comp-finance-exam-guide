Forward rates

What it is

The interest rate for a future period that is already implied by today's zero-coupon curve — the rate you could lock in now for borrowing/lending later.

Why it matters

The current curve doesn't just price today's loans — it embeds the market's view of future rates. Forward rates are used to price forwards, set mortgage rates starting in the future, and check for arbitrage between maturities.

How it works

No-arbitrage forces consistency: investing for \(T_2\) at the long rate must equal investing for \(T_1\) then rolling at the forward rate. With continuous compounding:

\[ r_{\text{fwd}}=\frac{R_2 T_2 - R_1 T_1}{T_2 - T_1} \]
  • R₁,R₂ zero rates for maturities T₁,T₂; the forward is the rate that covers the gap \([T_1,T_2]\).
Derivation Forward 1-year-in-1-year

Zero rates: 1y = 10.0%, 2y = 10.5%. Locking 1y now then a forward \(x\) for the 2nd year must match investing 2y outright:

\[ e^{0.10}\,e^{x}=e^{0.105\cdot2}\;\Rightarrow\;0.10+x=0.21\;\Rightarrow\;x=11\%. \]

If the forward were, say, 10%, you'd sell the 1y + forward and buy the 2y to earn \(100(e^{0.21}-e^{0.20})>0\) risk-free — an arbitrage. So 11% is forced. Generalising gives the formula above; the 1y-in-2y here works out to 11.4%.

Analogy

A forward rate is the price of a flight you book today for travel next year — set now from current information, even though you fly later. If two routes to the same destination cost different amounts, travellers arbitrage the gap away.

Worked example — mortgages

You'll buy a house in 1 year and want a 10-year mortgage. The bank prices it off the 11-year and 1-year rates — i.e. the forward 10-year rate starting in 1 year. That forward is baked into today's curve.

How the yield curve moves

How it works

The whole curve evolves — short end driven by central banks, the rest by market forces. Moves decompose into three classic shapes:

  • Parallel shift: the entire curve up or down by the same amount (duration captures this).
  • Steepening: long rates rise relative to short.
  • Flattening: long rates fall relative to short (can invert).
Analogy

Think of the curve as a see-saw plank: a parallel shift lifts the whole plank; steepening/flattening tilts it around a pivot.

Duration

What it is

The average time you wait to receive a bond's cash flows, weighted by their present value — and, conveniently, a measure of how sensitive the price is to yield.

Why it matters

Duration is the bond world's "Delta": one number summarising interest-rate risk. Portfolio managers control duration to dial risk up or down, especially when rates are rising.

How it works
\[ D=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(t_i-t)\,c_i\,e^{-y(t_i-t)}}{B(t)}=\sum_{i=1}^n (t_i-t)\cdot\underbrace{\frac{c_i e^{-y(t_i-t)}}{B(t)}}_{\text{PV weight, sums to 1}} \]

It's a weighted average of payment times, with weights = each cash flow's share of total present value. So a zero-coupon bond maturing in \(n\) years has duration exactly \(n\); a coupon bond has duration less than \(n\) (early coupons pull the average in).

Analogy

Duration is the balance point of a row of weights placed along a timeline — each weight is a discounted cash flow. A zero-coupon bond has all its weight at the end (balances at \(n\)); coupons add weights earlier, sliding the balance point left.

Duration as price sensitivity

How it works

Differentiate the price formula with respect to yield:

\[ \frac{\partial B}{\partial y}=-\sum_i (t_i-t)\,c_i\,e^{-y(t_i-t)} = -B\,D \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \boxed{\dfrac{\delta B}{B}=-D\,\delta y} \]

A small parallel yield shift \(\delta y\) changes price by approximately \(-D\,\delta y\) (in percent). Higher duration ⇒ bigger price swing. This is a first-order (linear) estimate — good for small shifts, less so for large ones.

Analogy

Duration is the bond's "leverage to rates" rating — like a car's 0-to-60 sensitivity. A duration-8 bond reacts roughly twice as hard to a rate move as a duration-4 bond.

Worked example — small vs large shift

Small shift \(\delta y=0.1\%\): estimate \(\delta B=-B D\delta y=-94.213\times2.653\times0.001=-0.250\). Actual recompute: \(93.963-94.213=-0.250\). ✓ Spot on.

Large shift \(\delta y=1\%\): estimate \(-94.213\times2.653\times0.01=-2.499\). Actual: \(91.749-94.213=-2.464\). The estimate overstates the loss — the linear approximation misses the curve. That gap is convexity.

📈What this shows: the true price–yield curve (solid) vs the straight-line duration estimate (dashed) tangent at y = 12%. They match for tiny moves but the line drifts below the curve for big moves — duration alone overestimates price drops and underestimates price rises. The curvature gap is convexity.

Convexity

What it is

The curvature of the price–yield relationship — the second-order correction that makes the estimate accurate for larger yield moves.

Why it matters

Convexity is a good thing for a bondholder: prices rise more when rates fall than they drop when rates rise by the same amount. Ignoring it makes you mis-estimate risk on big moves.

How it works
\[ C=\frac{1}{B}\frac{\partial^2 B}{\partial y^2}=\frac{1}{B}\sum_i c_i (t_i-t)^2 e^{-y(t_i-t)} \qquad \frac{\delta B}{B}\approx -D\,\delta y+\tfrac12 C\,(\delta y)^2 \]

The new \(+\tfrac12 C(\delta y)^2\) term is always positive (it lifts the price back toward the true curve), correcting duration's overshoot.

Analogy

Duration is the speed estimate; convexity is the "and you're also turning" correction. On a sharp bend (big rate move) you must account for the curve, not just the straight-line speed.

Worked example

Same bond, convexity \(C=7.570\), \(\delta y=1\%\): \(\delta B=-94.213\cdot2.653\cdot0.01+0.5\cdot94.213\cdot7.570\cdot0.01^2=-2.499+0.0357=-2.464\). Now it matches the true \(-2.464\). ✓ The convexity term closed the gap.

Types of duration

How it works
  • Macaulay duration (the one above): PV-weighted average time to cash flows.
  • Modified duration \(D_{\text{mod}}=\dfrac{D}{1+y/n_c}\): converts to a direct % price sensitivity per unit yield (with \(n_c\) compoundings/year).
  • Dollar duration / DV01 \(=\dfrac{D_{\text{mod}}B}{100}\): the cash price change for a 1bp (0.01%) yield move — the trader's working unit.
  • Effective duration: for bonds with embedded options (callable/putable) where cash flows aren't fixed.
  • Key-rate duration: sensitivity to specific points on the curve — captures non-parallel (steepening/flattening) shifts.

At portfolio level: duration is the weighted sum of constituent durations (per currency for multi-currency books). Managers keep duration down when rates are expected to rise. Volatility is a decent proxy for duration risk.

Examiner might ask Duration & convexity probes
Two meanings of duration — reconcile them.
It's both (a) the PV-weighted average time to cash flows and (b) the bond's price sensitivity to yield (\(\delta B/B=-D\,\delta y\)). They coincide because differentiating the PV pricing formula produces exactly that weighted-time expression.
Why does a coupon bond have lower duration than its maturity?
Coupons deliver some cash before maturity, placing PV weight at earlier times and pulling the weighted-average time below \(n\). A zero-coupon bond has all weight at the end, so its duration equals its maturity.
Duration estimate vs reality for a big rate move — which way is it wrong?
Duration alone overstates the price fall when rates rise and understates the gain when they fall, because the true relationship is convex. Add the \(+\tfrac12C(\delta y)^2\) convexity term to fix it.
Is convexity good or bad to own?
Good — for a given duration, more convexity means you gain more on rate falls and lose less on rate rises. Investors will pay (accept lower yield) for higher convexity.
One-line story for the exam "Today's curve implies forward rates by no-arbitrage. Duration is the PV-weighted average time to cash flows and equals the bond's first-order price sensitivity: \(\delta B/B=-D\,\delta y\). For big moves the price–yield curve bends, so we add the positive convexity term \(+\tfrac12 C\,\delta y^2\)."
