TP01 — Returns & Time Value of Money

↗ Theory: Returns & Time Value of Money

What you did

Computed simple returns and continuously-compounded (log) returns on price data, built a two-stock portfolio (Microsoft + Starbucks), and computed present/future values.

The logic
  • Simple return \(R=P_t/P_{t-1}-1\) compounds multiplicatively over time; log return \(r=\ln(P_t/P_{t-1})\) compounds additively and is symmetric.
  • Portfolio return is the weighted average of the asset returns, \(R_p=\sum_i w_i R_i\) — returns combine linearly across assets but multiplicatively across time.
  • Time value: \(FV=PV(1+R)^n\), and discounting reverses it; continuous compounding gives the \(e^{rn}\) limit.
Remember Log return is always slightly below the simple return (\(\ln(1+R)\lt R\)); use log returns for time aggregation, simple returns (weighted) for portfolios.
Examiner might ask TP01 probes
Why use log returns instead of simple returns?
They add over time (no products), are symmetric, and linearise the maths — ideal for modelling and aggregation. The downside: a portfolio's log return is not the weighted average of asset log returns, so for portfolios use simple returns.
How did you combine the two stocks?
Weighted average of their simple returns by capital allocation; the portfolio's expected return is \(\sum w_iE[R_i]\), but its risk needs the covariance (the topic of TP07).

TP02 — Correlation, hidden ACF, AR(1) & VaR

↗ Theory: Time Series & AR Models · Returns/risk

What you did

Four parts: (1) correlation of two constructed variables, (2) a sine wave hidden in noise recovered by the ACF, (3) an AR(1) model fitted to EUR/USD daily returns, (4) FX risk via EWMA volatility and 1-day Value-at-Risk.

The logic
  • Slope ≠ correlation: the regression slope is \(\beta=\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)/\mathrm{Var}(X)\), correlation is \(\rho=\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)/(\sigma_X\sigma_Y)\). They coincide only when \(\sigma_X=\sigma_Y\). (You found slope ≈ 0.28 vs ρ ≈ 0.79.)
  • Hidden sine: with signal-to-noise ≈ 1 the sine is invisible in the raw plot, but the ACF shows clean peaks at multiples of the period \(T\) — the whole point of looking at the ACF instead of the raw series.
  • AR(1) on returns: prices are non-stationary, returns are stationary; \(\varphi_1=\rho_1\approx-0.15\) (weak mean reversion). Since the ACF sits inside the confidence band, returns are ≈ white noise, so AR predictions are poor — that's the correct output, not a bug. Higher \(p\) doesn't help (no significant autocorrelation to exploit).
  • EWMA & VaR: \(\sigma_t^2=\lambda\sigma_{t-1}^2+(1-\lambda)r_{t-1}^2\) (RiskMetrics \(\lambda=0.94\)) reacts to recent returns; 1-day VaR \(=z_\alpha\,\sigma_t\,V_0\) with \(z_{99\%}=2.326,\ z_{95\%}=1.645\). The bands widen in volatile stretches.
Remember Near-zero, statistically-insignificant autocorrelation of returns is exactly what the weak-form EMH predicts — "the model failing to predict" is the finding. EWMA's \(\lambda\) sets memory: 0.94 ⇒ each new day gets 6% weight.
Examiner might ask TP02 probes
Difference between a regression slope and a correlation?
Both share the covariance in the numerator, but the slope divides by \(\mathrm{Var}(X)\) only, while correlation divides by both standard deviations (and is unit-free, in [−1,1]). Equal only if the two variances match.
Your AR(1) barely predicted anything — is that a failure?
No. \(\varphi_1\approx-0.15\) with an ACF inside the confidence band means the returns are essentially white noise. The model correctly says "tomorrow ≈ noise". Adding lags can't manufacture signal that isn't there.
What does VaR at 99% actually mean, and what's its key assumption here?
A loss you'd exceed only 1% of the time over one day. This parametric VaR assumes returns are normal with volatility \(\sigma_t\); fat tails mean it can understate true tail risk — a caveat worth stating.
Why EWMA over a simple rolling standard deviation?
EWMA weights recent observations more, so it reacts to volatility changes (clustering) instead of treating a shock from 50 days ago the same as yesterday's.

TP03 — Seasonality, AR(1) regimes & the Vasicek model

↗ Theory: Time Series · Markets (seasonality) · Bonds

What you did

Measured intraday/weekly seasonality in EUR/USD tick activity; simulated AR(1) across parameter regimes; rewrote the discrete Vasicek interest-rate model as an AR(1); and answered bonds-vs-stocks vocabulary.

The logic
  • Seasonality: binning ticks into 15-minute windows reveals a daily rhythm — quiet Asian session (≈22:00–06:00 UTC), pickup at the London open, and a peak around 13:00 UTC when London and New York overlap. This is a classic stylised fact.
  • AR(1) regimes driven by \(\varphi_1\): \(0.5\) stationary (shocks halve each step, fast mean reversion); \(0.98\) still stationary but "long memory" (slow decay, easily mistaken for a random walk); \(1.0\) random walk (shocks never decay — the classic stock-price model); \(1.05\) explosive (exponential divergence).
  • Vasicek: \(r_t-r_{t-1}=\kappa(\theta-r_{t-1})+\sigma\epsilon_t\) rearranges to \(r_t=\underbrace{\kappa\theta}_{c}+\underbrace{(1-\kappa)}_{\varphi_1}r_{t-1}+\sigma\epsilon_t\) — an AR(1) with drift. The rate is pulled back to the long-run level \(\theta\) like a rubber band; \(\kappa\) is the pull strength.
Remember \(|\varphi_1|\lt1\) = stationary, \(\varphi_1=1\) = unit root / random walk (non-stationary), \(\varphi_1\gt1\) = explosive. Mean reversion ⇔ \(0\lt\varphi_1\lt1\), and Vasicek's \(\varphi_1=1-\kappa\).
Examiner might ask TP03 probes
Given a value of \(\varphi_1\), what behaviour do you expect?
0<φ₁<1 → mean-reverting/stationary (smaller = faster reversion); φ₁≈1 → random walk (long wanders, no equilibrium); φ₁>1 → explosive growth; φ₁<0 → alternating/counter-trending.
How is Vasicek related to AR(1)?
It is an AR(1) with drift: \(\varphi_1=1-\kappa\), long-run mean \(\theta=c/(1-\varphi_1)\). κ controls how strongly the rate is pulled back to θ.
Why does FX activity peak mid-afternoon UTC?
The London–New York session overlap — the two largest trading centres are open simultaneously, maximising liquidity and tick frequency.

TP04 — Moving averages & intrinsic time

↗ Theory: Data, Moving Averages & Intrinsic Time

What you did

Implemented SMA and EMA and compared their smoothing/lag; computed mid-prices; detected directional changes for a threshold \(\delta\); and verified a scaling law on a log-log plot.

The logic
  • SMA vs EMA: close at small windows; as the window grows (or \(\alpha\) shrinks), the average is smoother but lags more. EMA reacts faster for the same smoothness because it weights recent data; \(\alpha=0.5\) ≈ raw data, \(\alpha=0.01\) very smooth and slow. Sanity checks: SMA(N=1)=series, EMA(α=1)=series.
  • Directional change: instead of sampling at fixed time, log an event when the price reverses by \(\delta\) from the last extreme. With \(\delta=1\%\) you get only ~15–20 events over two months — it filters out noise and keeps genuine reversals (intrinsic time).
  • Scaling law: count of directional changes vs \(\delta\) is a straight line on a log-log plot ⇒ a power law (≈ a million events at \(\delta=10^{-5}\), ≈ 20 at \(10^{-2}\)). This connects intrinsic time to physical time.
Remember Every moving average trades smoothness for lag — you cannot have both. A straight line on a log-log plot is the signature of a power-law (scaling) relationship.
Examiner might ask TP04 probes
SMA vs EMA — which and why?
EMA in practice: it's memory-light (one previous value), weights recent data, and is more responsive for a given smoothness. SMA is simpler but needs the full window and lags equally on all points.
What is a directional change and why use intrinsic time?
An event triggered when price reverses by δ from the last extreme. Intrinsic time samples by market activity rather than the arbitrary clock, so it's dense when volatile and ignores noise when calm.
Why is the log-log plot straight, and what does the slope mean?
Because the number of directional changes follows a power law in δ; on log-log a power law is a line whose slope is the scaling exponent linking the threshold to event frequency.

TP05 — Limit order book: the Maslov model

↗ Theory: Limit Order Book

What you did

Simulated the Maslov order-driven market (one trader per step; probability of buy/sell \(q\); probability of limit vs market order \(r\)), then studied returns, spread and book depth — and extended it with heterogeneous order sizes and aggressive/passive traders.

The logic
  • Mid vs market price: the market price only changes when a trade happens; the mid updates whenever a new limit order arrives — so they track but aren't identical.
  • Fat tails: the return histogram is far from normal — a tall thin centre with heavy tails (excess kurtosis ≈ 13). Big jumps occur when a market order eats through to a far limit order.
  • What it reproduces vs misses: fat tails ✓ and near-zero return autocorrelation ✓, but no real volatility clustering (a known limitation of the basic model).
  • Spread & depth: the spread widens when market orders consume the book faster than limit orders refill it; depth is a hump peaked near the best quote (because \(K=1\) tick).
  • Parameters: more limit orders (\(r\to0.9\)) → deeper, more liquid book, lower volatility; more market orders (\(r\to0.1\)) → higher volatility. Heterogeneous order sizes widen price swings but actually reduce kurtosis (impact spread across levels).
Remember A very simple model reproduces fat tails and (lack of) autocorrelation, but not volatility clustering — that's the gap that motivates richer agent-based models.
Examiner might ask TP05 probes
What stylised facts does Maslov reproduce, and which does it miss?
Reproduces: fat-tailed returns and near-zero return autocorrelation. Misses: volatility clustering (and realistic long-memory) — the model is too homogeneous.
Why do the mid and market prices differ?
The mid is (bid+ask)/2 and moves on any new best quote (limit order); the market/transaction price only updates when an actual trade executes.
How does the limit/market mix (\(r\)) change the market?
More limit orders deepen the book and damp volatility (liquidity); more market orders thin it and raise volatility and spread.

TP06 — Execution strategies

↗ Theory: Algorithmic Execution

What you did

Executed a 12 M EUR buy on EUR/USD over 20 random 3-hour windows using TWAP, VWAP, and a price-adaptive directional-change (DC) strategy, benchmarked against the decision price, and tested volatility sensitivity.

The logic
  • TWAP: 12 equal slices every 15 min. Mean cost +2.60 pips vs the decision price (the market trended up, so spreading the buy paid progressively more), with a large std (~16.7 pips) — the result depends heavily on luck.
  • VWAP: slices weighted by a volume proxy (tick count). It came out nearly identical to TWAP because EUR/USD tick activity was fairly uniform across the windows, so the weights ≈ equal.
  • Directional-change strategy: buy a slice each time the ask falls by \(\delta\). Best performer — mean −0.57 pips (paid less than the decision price) and a much lower std (~5.4 pips). The catch is choosing \(\delta\): too small fires everything at once, too large risks not finishing.
  • Volatility: TWAP/VWAP cost dispersion stays high in all regimes (rigid schedules don't adapt); the adaptive DC strategy's dispersion grows when volatility is high.
Remember The benchmark is the decision price (implementation shortfall). Rigid schedules (TWAP/VWAP) are predictable and luck-dependent; price-adaptive execution can beat them on average but adds parameter risk.
Examiner might ask TP06 probes
Why were TWAP and VWAP almost identical for you?
VWAP only differs from TWAP through the volume profile. EUR/USD tick activity was roughly uniform across the windows, so the volume weights collapsed to near-equal — making VWAP behave like TWAP.
What is the decision price and why benchmark against it?
The price at the instant you decided to trade (here \(\text{ask}(t_0)\)). The gap between your average fill and that price is implementation shortfall — the true cost of execution, capturing impact + timing.
Trade-off of the adaptive (DC) strategy?
It can secure a better average price by waiting for dips, but it's sensitive to the threshold δ and to volatility — there's a risk of not completing the order, i.e. opportunity cost.

TP07 — Optimal portfolio (Markowitz)

↗ Theory: Portfolio Construction (MPT)

What you did

Built the Markowitz efficient frontier for 7 stocks three ways — Monte-Carlo cloud, closed-form analytical solution, and a constrained optimiser — and located the minimum-variance portfolio with and without short-selling.

The logic
  • Metrics: \(\mu_p=w^\top\mu,\ \sigma_p=\sqrt{w^\top C w}\). 100k random weight vectors trace a bullet/parabola cloud in risk-return space.
  • Analytical frontier: the closed-form Lagrange solution \(w^*=C^{-1}(\lambda_1\mathbf1+\lambda_2\mu)\) hugs the left edge of the cloud. Only the upper half (above the min-variance point) is efficient.
  • Minimum-variance portfolio: \(w_{\text{gmv}}=C^{-1}\mathbf1/(\mathbf1^\top C^{-1}\mathbf1)\) — pure risk minimisation, so it can carry a slightly negative return and concentrate in the lowest-variance names.
  • No-short constraint: a convex quadratic program. Here the constrained and unconstrained frontiers were almost identical because the unconstrained optimum already had non-negative weights — the constraint was non-binding (dataset-specific, not a general rule).
Remember Three routes to the same frontier: Monte Carlo (brute force), closed-form (exact, allows shorts), constrained optimiser (realistic). A constraint only changes the answer if it actually binds.
Examiner might ask TP07 probes
Why does the Monte-Carlo cloud have a parabola/bullet shape?
Portfolio variance is quadratic in the weights, so the achievable set is bounded by a hyperbola in (σ, return) space; the left boundary is the efficient frontier.
What is the minimum-variance portfolio and why can its return be negative?
The leftmost frontier point — it minimises risk regardless of return, so if low-variance assets happen to have poor returns, the GMV return can be small or negative.
When does a no-short-selling constraint matter?
Only when the unconstrained optimum wants negative weights. If the optimal weights are already non-negative (as in your data), the constraint is non-binding and the frontier is unchanged.

TP08 — The Minority Game

↗ Theory: Agent-Based Models

What you did

Implemented the Minority Game with softmax strategy selection and studied how the scaled volatility of attendance, \(\sigma^2/N\), depends on the control parameter \(\alpha=2^M/N\), locating the critical point.

The logic
  • Why "minority": attendance \(A(t)=\sum_i a_i(t)\); if \(A\gt0\) then \(+1\) is the majority, so the \(-1\) minority wins. The payoff rule rewards being on the smaller side.
  • Rationality \(\Gamma\): agents pick strategies by softmax over accumulated utilities; large \(\Gamma\) → greedy (always the best-scoring strategy), small \(\Gamma\) → near-random.
  • Phase behaviour: small \(\alpha\) (many agents vs few strategies) → herding and high variance (worse than random); large \(\alpha\) → effectively random, variance → 1; there's a minimum near the critical \(\alpha_c\) (theory ≈ 0.34 for \(S=2\)).
  • You honestly noted your measured \(\alpha_c\) deviated from theory due to slow learning settings (small \(\Gamma\), short runs) — a convergence issue, not a conceptual one.
Remember The σ²/N-vs-α curve has a minimum at the critical point: below it the crowd herds (inefficient, worse than random), above it agents behave randomly. It's a genuine phase transition.
Examiner might ask TP08 probes
Why is it called the minority game and how does it map to markets?
The minority side wins each round — like a crowded trade: if everyone buys, there's no one left to buy from, so the majority loses. It models why being with the crowd is self-defeating.
What does the control parameter α capture?
α = 2ᴹ/N, the ratio of strategy-space size to number of agents. Small α → strategy overlap and herding; large α → diverse, near-random behaviour. The transition is at α_c ≈ 0.34 (S=2).
Your α_c didn't match theory — why?
A convergence artifact: with a small rationality Γ and short simulations, agents barely learn, so the herding regime is under-developed. The qualitative minimum is still there; the exact location needs longer runs.

TP09 — Option pricing: Black–Scholes vs Binomial tree

↗ Theory: Options — Basics

What you did

Priced a European call with Black–Scholes (\(S_0=100,K=120,T=1,\sigma=20\%,r=5\%\)), built a Cox-Ross-Rubinstein binomial tree, studied its convergence, then back-solved implied volatility from tree prices by bisection.

The logic
  • Benchmark: BS gives \(C\approx3.25\); the option is out-of-the-money (\(K\gt S_0\)) so most value is time value.
  • Binomial (CRR): \(u=e^{\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}},\ d=1/u\), risk-neutral \(p\), priced by backward induction. It converges to BS as \(N\to\infty\) — but the convergence oscillates (a known CRR feature), so you need \(N\approx200\text{–}500\) for a stable error.
  • Implied vol by bisection: BS price is strictly increasing in \(\sigma\) (Vega > 0), so bisection is guaranteed to converge to the σ that reproduces a given price.
  • "Smile" was a numerical artifact: the tree prices were generated with a flat \(\sigma=0.20\), so any curvature in implied-vol-vs-strike is pure discretisation error (a skew at low \(N\)), flattening to 0.20 as \(N\) grows — not an economic smile.
Remember The binomial tree → Black–Scholes in the limit (it discretises the same geometric Brownian motion). A flat-vol model can still produce a fake smile from numerical error — don't confuse that with the real, market-driven smile (fat tails).
Examiner might ask TP09 probes
Why does the binomial tree converge to Black–Scholes?
As steps increase, the CRR up/down process approximates geometric Brownian motion in continuous time, and the discounted risk-neutral expectation converges to the BS formula. Convergence is non-monotone (oscillates), so use a large N.
Why is bisection guaranteed to find the implied volatility?
Because the BS price is a strictly increasing (monotonic) function of σ — Vega is positive — so the price-minus-target function changes sign exactly once and bisection brackets it.
You got a smile from a flat-vol model — explain.
It's a discretisation artifact of the coarse tree, not economics. Since the prices came from a single σ=0.20, the true implied-vol curve is flat; the apparent skew vanishes as N→∞. A real market smile instead reflects fat tails / stochastic volatility.

TP10 — The Greeks

↗ Theory: Options — The Greeks

What you did

Plotted price, Delta and Gamma for both calls and puts as functions of the spot \(S_0\) (60–200), with \(K=120,\sigma=20\%,r=5\%,T=1\).

The logic
  • Price: call value rises with \(S_0\) (the right to buy gets more valuable), put value falls.
  • Delta: call Delta \(=N(d_1)\) runs 0→1; put Delta \(=N(d_1)-1\) runs −1→0 — the put curve is the call curve shifted down by 1 (put–call parity).
  • Gamma: identical for a call and a put at the same strike, always positive, and peaks at the money — that's where Delta changes fastest and hedging is hardest.
Remember Call and put share the same Gamma (differentiate put–call parity twice — the linear \(S\) and discounted-\(K\) terms vanish). Delta is the hedge ratio; Gamma is why a delta hedge must be rebalanced.
Examiner might ask TP10 probes
Why do a call and a put on the same underlying have the same Gamma?
From put–call parity \(c-p=S-Ke^{-rT}\): differentiate twice w.r.t. \(S\). The right side is linear in \(S\), so its second derivative is 0 ⇒ \(\Gamma_c=\Gamma_p\). (Their Deltas differ by exactly 1, for the same reason.)
Where is Gamma largest and why does it matter?
At the money, increasing as expiry nears. That's where Delta is most sensitive to price, so a delta hedge needs the most frequent rebalancing — the writer is "short gamma".
What's the range of Delta and what does it represent?
Call Delta ∈ (0,1), put Delta ∈ (−1,0). It's the hedge ratio (shares per option) and approximately the risk-neutral probability of finishing in the money.
