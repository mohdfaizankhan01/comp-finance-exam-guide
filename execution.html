Algorithmic Execution

You want to buy a lot of shares without shoving the price up against yourself. These are the algorithms that slice big orders to minimise cost — and how to choose between them.

The execution problem

What it is

Deciding how to trade a large order — split into how many pieces, at what times — so the total cost (including the price you yourself push) is as small as possible.

Why it matters

For a big institution, execution cost can dwarf research alpha. Buying a full day's volume in one go could move the price several percent — that's real money lost before the position even works.

How it works

Four cost sources to balance:

  • Impact: trading fast pushes the price (you don't want to buy yourself up).
  • Timing: if you trade more than once, when?
  • Delay: trade too slowly and your idea/edge may decay.
  • Opportunity: can you even get all the shares?
Analogy

Like selling a huge stash of concert tickets: dump them all at once and the price collapses (impact); dribble them out too slowly and the concert's over (delay). The art is the pace in between.

Worked example

You want 10,000 shares of a stock whose entire daily volume is 10,000. Buy it all now and you'll badly move the price; you must spread it across the day (and maybe across days), accepting some delay risk.

Market impact

How it works

Impact is the price change your own order causes. It splits into:

  • Temporary impact: the price dips/jumps while you trade, then recovers.
  • Permanent impact: a lasting shift, because your order leaked information or triggered other traders.

It's hard to measure (you'd need the counterfactual "what if I hadn't traded"). As seen in the LOB lecture, instantaneous impact is concave in size — so splitting helps.

Analogy

Temporary impact is the splash when you jump in a pool (settles quickly); permanent impact is the ripple others mistake for a current and start swimming with — it doesn't fully fade.

The algorithm zoo

How it works
AlgoIdeaStrength / weakness
TWAPTime-Weighted: equal slices at equal time intervals.Dead simple; ignores price & volume; highly predictable.
VWAPVolume-Weighted: trade in proportion to the day's volume profile (\(\text{VWAP}=\frac{\sum p_i v_i}{\sum v_i}\)).Tracks a fair benchmark; needs historical volume pattern (total volume only known at day's end).
POVPercentage Of Volume: trade \(w_i=\alpha\, v_i\) of each interval's volume.Adapts to live volume; still predictable; no price logic.
ISImplementation Shortfall: minimise gap between execution price and the decision price.Balances impact vs timing risk; uses risk aversion; front-loads trading.
MI / MC / PI / LIQMinimal Impact (dark pools), Market Close, Price Inline, Liquidity-driven.Specialised objectives.

TWAP is about trading regularly; VWAP adds trading in the right proportions. Both are predictable, so practitioners add noise to timing/sizes to hide. POV self-adjusts: subtract your own participation to get \(\tilde\alpha=\alpha/(1-\alpha)\).

📈What this shows: % of the order completed over the day. TWAP is a straight line (constant pace). VWAP/POV follows the U-shaped daily volume — trading more at the busy open and close, less at the quiet midday — to blend in with natural flow.
Analogy

TWAP = eating the same amount every hour regardless. VWAP = eating when everyone else eats (lunch rush) so your portion goes unnoticed in the crowd.

Efficient frontier & risk aversion

What it is

A way to choose among algorithms: plot each one's expected cost against its risk (cost variability, mostly timing). The best ones form a frontier; your risk appetite picks the point.

Why it matters

It mirrors Markowitz exactly — there's no single "best" algorithm, only the cheapest one for a given level of risk. It makes the choice principled rather than ad hoc.

How it works

Trading fast → low timing risk but high impact cost (e.g. Market Close). Trading slow → low impact but high timing risk (e.g. Minimal Impact). The trade-off traces an efficient frontier; for a given risk you pick the lowest-cost algo. Define risk aversion \(\lambda=\text{cost}/\text{risk}\): high λ = cautious (accept cost to cut risk), low λ = aggressive.

📈What this shows: the cost–risk plane for execution algos. Market Close (MC) sits low-risk/high-cost; Minimal Impact (MI) high-risk/low-cost; IS, POV, VWAP fill the curve. You choose the point matching your risk aversion λ — exactly like picking a portfolio on the efficient frontier.
Analogy

Like choosing a delivery speed: overnight (low risk it's late, high cost) vs standard (cheap, but risk of delay). Your patience (risk aversion λ) decides which shipping option is "best" for you.

Portfolio note Trading a whole portfolio (many assets at once) needs extra care: orders must stay aligned to keep diversification intact during execution — "algorithms of algorithms".
Examiner might ask Execution probes
TWAP vs VWAP?
TWAP slices equally over time, ignoring volume — simple but naïve and predictable. VWAP trades in proportion to the volume profile, blending into natural flow and tracking a fairer benchmark, but it relies on the volume pattern (true total is only known at close).
Why split a large order at all?
To minimise market impact, which is concave but partly permanent. Trading slowly cuts impact but raises timing risk (the price may drift away) — the central trade-off.
What is Implementation Shortfall minimising?
The gap between the average execution price and the price at the moment you decided to trade — balancing impact against timing risk, tuned by the investor's risk aversion.
How do you choose an algorithm?
Pick from the cost–risk efficient frontier the point matching your risk aversion λ = cost/risk: aggressive (low λ) tolerates impact to reduce timing risk; cautious (high λ) does the opposite.
One-line story for the exam "Big orders move the price (concave, partly-permanent impact), so we slice them: TWAP (by time), VWAP/POV (by volume), IS (minimise slippage vs the decision price). Trading fast cuts timing risk but costs impact and vice-versa, tracing an efficient frontier of algorithms — you pick the point that matches your risk aversion λ."
