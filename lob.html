Limit Order Book

The engine inside an exchange: a live list of all resting buy and sell orders. Understanding it is the key to execution, impact, and market liquidity.

Two ways to trade

What it is

You either trade against a market maker (OTC) who quotes you a price, or you post into a limit order book (LOB) on an exchange where buyers and sellers are matched directly.

Why it matters

Most modern trading happens through LOBs. They're where price is actually formed, where liquidity lives, and where your own orders can move the market against you.

How it works

A market maker holds an inventory (hence risk) and profits from the spread — buying at the bid, selling at the ask — sometimes hedging with another maker when too exposed. A LOB instead collects everyone's orders and matches them algorithmically: it's the meeting place of all participants.

Analogy

A market maker is a currency-exchange booth (you take their quote; they pocket the spread). A LOB is an eBay for one product — a live list of everyone's bids and asks, matched automatically.

Definitions: bid, ask, spread, depth

How it works

The LOB \(L(t)\) is the set of all active orders at time \(t\). An order is \(x=(p_x,w_x,t_x)\): price, size, insertion time.

  • Bid \(b(t)=\max_{x\in B(t)}p_x\) — the highest price someone will buy at.
  • Ask \(a(t)=\min_{x\in A(t)}p_x\) — the lowest price someone will sell at.
  • Spread \(s(t)=a(t)-b(t)\); mid-price \(m(t)=\tfrac{a(t)+b(t)}2\).
  • Tick \(\pi\): smallest price increment. Depth \(N(p,t)\): total size resting at each price level.

An order that matches immediately is a market order; one that rests in the book is a limit order.

📊What this shows: a snapshot of book depth. Green bars (bids) sit below, red bars (asks) above; the empty gap between the best bid and best ask is the spread. Taller bars = more liquidity resting at that price.
Analogy

The book is a staircase of orders on each side; the spread is the empty landing in the middle, and depth is how many people are queued on each step.

How the book evolves

How it works

For an incoming buy order at price \(p_x\):

  • \(p_x\le b(t)\): rests deep — limit order, best bid unchanged.
  • \(b(t)<p_x<a(t)\): limit order that improves the bid up to \(p_x\) (thinner spread).
  • \(p_x\ge a(t)\): market order — matched immediately at the best ask.

Limit orders get a better price but carry opportunity risk: they might never execute. That's the eternal trade-off — cheaper fill vs certainty of fill.

Analogy

A limit order is leaving a "I'll buy at this price" note and waiting; a market order is grabbing whatever's on the shelf right now. The note is cheaper but might be ignored forever.

Worked example

Book at bid 1.50 / ask 1.53. A limit buy at 1.51 lifts the bid to 1.51 (spread shrinks 0.03→0.02). A market buy at 1.53 hits the ask, lifting it to the next level (1.54) — the spread and mid both move.

Priority rules

How it works

When several orders sit at the same best price, who gets filled first?

  • Price-time: best price first; ties broken by who arrived first → rewards posting early.
  • Pro-rata: best price; ties split proportionally to size (\(w/\sum w\)) → rewards large orders.
  • Price-size: best price; largest order first → also rewards size.
Analogy

Price-time is a first-come queue at a bakery; pro-rata is splitting the last loaves by how much each person ordered; price-size is serving the biggest order first.

Empirical facts & market impact

How it works

Across markets (with variation): order sizes cluster at round numbers; the relative price of orders (distance from best) is fat-tailed (orders placed far away); 70–80% of orders are cancelled; book depth is fat-tailed.

Market impact: an order moves the price. Impact = temporary (recovers) + permanent (sticks, because the order carried information or triggered others). Crucially, instantaneous price impact \(I(|w|)\) is a concave function of size — doubling the order less than doubles the impact (Lillo et al. found a "master curve"). This is why big orders are split up.

Analogy

Buying a lot is like drinking through a straw from a pond: the first gulps barely lower the level (concave impact), but each gulp leaves a small permanent dip — others notice and react.

Why it matters Concave, partly-permanent impact is the entire reason for the next lecture (algorithmic execution): break big orders into pieces to minimise how much you push the price against yourself.

The Maslov model

How it works

A famously simple physicist's model (Maslov, 2000) of an order-driven market:

  • One trader per step; 50/50 buyer or seller.
  • With probability \(1-r\): submit a market order; otherwise a limit order at last price ± a random offset \(k\).
  • No cancellations.

Despite its crudeness, it reproduces fat-tailed returns and negative short-lag autocorrelation — and a Hurst exponent of ¼ (vs ½ for a random walk), signalling anti-persistent memory. Impressive structure from almost nothing.

Analogy

Like discovering that a few simple traffic rules generate realistic, complex jams — minimal ingredients, surprisingly lifelike behaviour. (Heterogeneous agents come next, in agent-based models.)

Examiner might ask LOB probes
Define bid, ask, spread, mid.
Bid = highest buy price, ask = lowest sell price, spread = ask − bid, mid = their average. The spread is the immediate round-trip cost of liquidity.
Market vs limit order — the trade-off?
Market order: immediate fill, pays the spread/impact. Limit order: better price, but opportunity risk of never executing. Choose based on urgency.
Why is market impact concave, and why does it matter?
Empirically \(I(|w|)\) grows slower than linearly in size; combined with a permanent component, it means large orders should be split and worked over time — the basis of execution algorithms.
What's surprising about the Maslov model?
Extremely simple rules (random market/limit orders, no cancellation) already reproduce fat tails and negative short-lag autocorrelation — real stylised facts emerge from minimal structure.
One-line story for the exam "A limit order book is the live list of resting buy/sell orders; the best bid and ask define the spread, market orders cross it while limit orders rest with opportunity risk, and orders are matched by priority rules. Real books show fat tails, ~80% cancellations and concave, partly-permanent market impact — which is exactly why big orders must be split (next: execution)."
