Agent-Based Models

Instead of writing one equation for the market, you simulate many simple traders and watch realistic complexity emerge from the bottom up.

Top-down vs bottom-up

What it is

Agent-Based Models (ABMs) build a market from many interacting "agents" following simple rules, rather than describing the market with one global equation.

Why it matters

Markets are the result of interactions, not a tidy formula. ABMs explain where the stylised facts (fat tails, volatility clustering) come from — emergent behaviour you can't get from a top-down equation.

How it works
  • Top-down: write the equation of the dynamics directly (e.g. an SDE).
  • Bottom-up (ABM): specify simple agent rules; run the simulation; complex macro behaviour emerges.

Classic intuition pump: simple car-following rules reproduce real traffic jams — micro simplicity → macro complexity.

Analogy

A flock of birds has no leader and no flock-equation — just each bird following 3 rules about its neighbours. The mesmerising murmuration emerges. ABMs do the same for markets.

Cont-Bouchaud — herding

How it works

Agents form random clusters that act together. Each cluster buys with probability \(a\), sells with prob. \(a\), or does nothing with prob. \(1-2a\) (with \(a\le0.5\)).

  • \(a=0.5\) (no inactivity) → Gaussian returns.
  • \(a\ll0.5\) (lots of coordinated inaction, strong clustering) → fat-tailed returns.

So a single herding parameter dials between normal and fat-tailed markets — herding creates the fat tails.

Analogy

Like a crowd in a stadium: when people act independently, the noise is mild and Gaussian. When they coordinate (a chant, a wave), you get sudden huge surges — fat tails.

Lux-Marchesi — fundamentalists vs chartists

How it works

Two types of agent, who can switch type:

  • Fundamentalists: compare price to a "true" value — buy if too cheap, sell if too dear.
  • Chartists: follow price action and neighbours — optimists chase trends, pessimists fade them.

The interplay (and switching) reproduces fat-tailed return distributions and is somewhat at odds with EMH (price doesn't just reflect news). It captures booms/busts as chartists pile into trends until fundamentalists pull price back.

Analogy

A housing market with value-investors ("this is overpriced, I'll sell") and momentum-buyers ("prices keep rising, get in!"). When everyone flips to momentum, you get a bubble; fundamentalists eventually burst it.

The Minority Game

What it is

A model (Challet-Zhang, Fribourg) where many agents repeatedly choose one of two sides, and the minority side wins — capturing the self-defeating nature of crowded trades.

Why it matters

It's analytically tractable and reproduces real features (herding, realistic return distributions). It formalises why "if everyone buys, there's no one left to buy from" — the loser is the crowd.

How it works

Inspired by the El Farol Bar problem: \(N\) people decide independently whether to go to a bar that's only fun if <60% attend. Globally you want exactly 60% — but everyone reasoning the same way causes over/under-shooting.

In the MG: \(N\) (odd) agents, a history \(\mu(t)\) of \(M\) bits, \(S\) strategies each (there are \(2^{2^M}\) possible strategies — explodes fast). Each step agents act \(a_i=\pm1\); attendance \(A=\sum a_i\); strategies that predicted the minority are rewarded. A control parameter \(\alpha=2^M/N\) governs the phases.

Result Fluctuations of attendance

If agents were random and independent: \(E[a_i]=0\), \(\mathrm{Var}(a_i)=1\), and with zero covariance \(\mathrm{Var}(A)=N\). The interesting physics is the departure from this: below a critical \(\alpha_c\approx0.337\) (for \(S=2\)) the system is in a symmetric, crowded phase (worse than random — herding); above it, an asymmetric phase where the history becomes predictable. A "master curve" of volatility vs \(\alpha\) appears for various \(N\).

Linked to markets via \(r(t)=\ln\frac{P(t)}{P(t-1)}=A(t)/\lambda\), i.e. \(P(t)=P(t-1)e^{A(t)/\gamma}\). Add contrarians/trend-followers and it reproduces return-distribution features.

📈What this shows: a stylised Minority-Game volatility-of-attendance vs the control parameter α = 2ᴹ/N. There's a critical point α_c ≈ 0.337: to its left, crowding makes fluctuations worse than random (herding); near α_c is the optimal, lowest-volatility zone.
Analogy

It's the "pick the less crowded checkout lane" game played by everyone at once. If you all use the same logic, you all switch to the same lane and lose. Winning means being in the minority — which is exactly why crowded trades fail.

Examiner might ask ABM probes
What do agent-based models add over top-down equations?
They explain emergence: simple heterogeneous agents reproduce stylised facts (fat tails, volatility clustering) from the bottom up, rather than assuming them in an equation.
In one line, each of the three models?
Cont-Bouchaud: random clusters → herding produces fat tails. Lux-Marchesi: fundamentalists vs switching chartists → bubbles & fat tails. Minority Game: minority-wins game → why crowded trades lose, with a phase transition at α_c.
What's the El Farol connection?
The bar is fun only if under-attended; everyone deciding simultaneously with similar logic over/under-shoots the 60% target. The Minority Game abstracts this into "be on the minority side to win" — a market metaphor.
Why is the Minority Game prized?
It's one of the few ABMs yielding analytical results (phase transition at α_c, master curves), while still generating realistic market statistics — and it has spawned many variants.
One-line story for the exam "Agent-based models build markets bottom-up from simple traders, so stylised facts emerge. Cont-Bouchaud gets fat tails from herding clusters; Lux-Marchesi from fundamentalists vs chartists; and the analytically-tractable Minority Game (minority wins) explains why crowded trades self-destruct, with a phase transition at α_c ≈ 0.337."
