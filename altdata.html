Alternative Data

Non-traditional data — satellite images, social media, card transactions — used to gain an edge. Promising, messy, and still early-stage.

What it is & why use it

What it is

Data that isn't the usual price/fundamental feed — satellite imagery, social media, GPS/location, web traffic, consumer transactions, ship movements, IoT sensors.

Why it matters

It can give orthogonal insight (uncorrelated with what everyone else sees), be anticipatory (lead official numbers, like PMI vs GDP), or be complementary (e.g. CO₂ data). Edge comes from seeing what isn't yet in the price.

How it works

No crisp definition, but alt data tends to be: less commonly used, more costly, sourced outside financial markets, with shorter history and harder to use. "Exhaust data" (a by-product of some activity, e.g. trading logs) is one flavour. Only ~1% of available data is currently analysed (McKinsey) — held back by cost, process change and hiring.

Analogy

Everyone reads the same newspaper (price data). Alt data is counting the cars in the mall car park from a satellite before the sales figures are published — you see the story early, from a different angle.

Risks & challenges

How it works

Risks: legal (personal-data laws vary by region; web scraping may violate terms or use non-public data; contractual limits), quality/validity (noisy tweets, faulty sensors), and vendor risk (data providers can shut down, sometimes due to legal change).

Challenges: data is unstructured (text, images, video, even handwriting; often no timestamp), riddled with missing values and outliers, hard to match to the right entity, and must be cleaned and aggregated into regular time series by ticker/category.

Analogy

Alt data is ore, not gold. There's value in there, but it arrives full of dirt (noise), in odd shapes (unstructured), and you need legal permission to mine it — refining it is most of the work.

Outliers nuance "One man's noise is another man's signal." An outlier (an abnormal card transaction, the Jan 2015 EUR/CHF floor break) might be an error to delete — or the very signal you want. Treating outliers is genuinely tricky.

The process behind usage

How it works
  1. Set vision & strategy (papers, small proof-of-concept).
  2. Identify datasets (data scouting).
  3. Vendor due diligence (will they survive?).
  4. Pre-assess risks (tech, legal, cyber).
  5. Pre-assess signal on a sample (POC).
  6. Onboard the data → preprocess → extract signal.
  7. Productionise → maintain.

It needs new roles — data scouts, strategists, engineers, scientists — and demand is rising. Machine learning is the workhorse for turning unstructured data into signals.

Analogy

Like opening a new mine: survey the land, check you're allowed to dig, test a sample for gold, then build the full extraction pipeline — and keep maintaining it.

Applications

How it works
  • Missing-data / outliers: imputation and anomaly detection (KNN, LOF, autoencoders) as essential first steps.
  • Satellite imagery: counting cars in store car parks to estimate earnings; explaining export growth.
  • Location data: GPS footfall scores vs earnings estimates (e.g. Thasos index).
  • Text/social/news: sentiment from Twitter (Hedonometer happiness index regressed on the S&P), Twitter-enhanced payroll forecasts, news-based currency scores.
  • Investor attention: Google Trends, Investopedia Anxiety Index (adds value beyond the VIX).
  • Consumer transactions: credit-card panels (Mastercard SpendingPulse), anonymised receipts.
Analogy

Each source is a different sense: satellites are eyes on the ground, social media is the crowd's mood, card data is the economy's pulse — combined, they sketch the picture before the official report lands.

Examiner might ask Alt-data probes
What makes data "alternative", and why use it?
It's non-traditional (satellite, social, transactions), costly, often unstructured, with short history. The value is orthogonal, anticipatory, or complementary insight — an edge from information not yet in the price.
Give a concrete successful example.
Counting cars in retailer car parks via satellite to forecast quarterly earnings ahead of the official release; or Twitter sentiment predicting NFP surprises.
Main challenges?
Legal/privacy risk, vendor risk, and heavy data-engineering: unstructured formats, missing values, outliers, entity matching, and aggregation into clean time series.
Is more alt data always better?
No — as one CEO (Man Group) noted, data grows faster than the resources to exploit it, so they used less over time. Signal extraction and process discipline matter more than volume. But firms ignoring it risk being outmanoeuvred (Deloitte).
One-line story for the exam "Alternative data — satellite, social, location, transactions — offers orthogonal, anticipatory insight before it hits official numbers. It's powerful but early-stage: unstructured, costly, legally fraught and full of outliers, so most of the work is a disciplined pipeline (scout → vet → POC → onboard → extract signal with ML → maintain)."
