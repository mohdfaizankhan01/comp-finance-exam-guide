Markets & Behaviours

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)

What it is

The claim (Fama, 1960s; Nobel 2013) that prices already reflect all available information, so you can't systematically beat the market — prices are always "right".

Why it matters

It's the central debate of finance. If EMH holds, active management and technical analysis are pointless and you should index. If it fails, there's structure to exploit — the entire quant industry bets that it does fail, at least a little.

How it works

Three nested forms, by what "all information" includes:

  • Weak: prices reflect all past price data → technical analysis is useless; price is a random walk driven only by new (exogenous) information.
  • Semi-strong: prices instantly reflect all public information (news, fundamentals) → fundamental analysis of public data won't give an edge.
  • Strong: prices reflect all information, public and private → even insiders can't profit.
Analogy

EMH says the market is a classroom where everyone instantly shares every answer. The moment a fact is known, it's priced — so there's no leftover "easy mark" to grab.

Evidence against

Markets look far from fully rational: prolonged over-valuations vs fundamentals, herding, the persistent success of a Buffett or Soros (luck?), the 2010 Flash Crash, and the Bitcoin bubble. The weak form is criticised even on its own turf.

Examiner might ask EMH probes
State the three forms and what each rules out.
Weak (past prices priced in → no technical analysis), semi-strong (all public info priced in → no fundamental edge), strong (public + private priced in → even insider info useless).
How do bubbles and crashes square with EMH?
They're hard to reconcile — sustained mispricing (Bitcoin) and liquidity-driven crashes (Flash Crash) suggest prices aren't always "right", challenging even the weak form.
If markets were strong-form efficient, why do quants exist?
Because they bet efficiency is imperfect and transient — small, exploitable structure (the stylised facts below) survives long enough to trade, even if classic "free money" doesn't.

Where we trade

How it works
  • Regulated exchanges (NYSE, LSE, CME, SIX): a defined, strictly-regulated venue with a clearing process; increasingly electronic. Most volume happens here.
  • OTC (Over-The-Counter) (e.g. Oanda): less formal, riskier, run by market makers who quote prices and carry an inventory; no central transparency, not necessarily a physical place.
  • Market maker (Oanda) holds inventory ⇒ takes risk ⇒ is paid via the spread. A broker (Dukascopy) just routes orders ⇒ no inventory, no price risk.
Analogy

An exchange is a regulated farmers' market with a referee; OTC is haggling with a dealer in their shop — more flexible, less transparent, and you trust the dealer's quote.

Assets & main instruments

How it works

Asset classes: equity (dividends), bonds (loans to states/companies), commodities (wheat, oil — via futures), currencies (traded in pairs, with an interest-rate differential). Currencies are the backbone — the FX market is the largest, ~$5 trillion/day, a quarter of it EUR/USD.

Instruments: spot (the thing itself), forward (agree to trade later at a set price), future (standardised, exchange-traded forward), option (a right, not obligation, to trade), index (a basket like the S&P 500), and other derivatives. Futures are rolled before delivery to stay exposed without receiving the physical wheat; options and futures are also used to hedge.

Analogy

Spot is buying apples today; a forward/future is pre-agreeing next month's apple price; an option is paying for the right to buy apples at a set price; an index is a fruit basket you trade as one unit.

Stylised facts — why prices aren't a random walk

What it is

Robust statistical regularities seen across virtually all markets that a simple Gaussian random walk fails to reproduce.

Why it matters

They're the fingerprints of real markets (human + algorithmic interaction). They directly challenge EMH-style randomness and motivate richer models (GARCH, agent-based models) and risk management — fat tails mean disasters are more common than a bell curve predicts.

How it works
  • Volatility clustering: big moves follow big moves, calm follows calm (heteroskedasticity — the "H" in GARCH).
  • Fat tails: extreme returns are far more likely than the normal distribution says.
  • Negative autocorrelation at short lags (high frequency).
  • Scaling laws: self-similar behaviour across timescales, e.g. \(E[|r|]\sim\Delta t^{\,\alpha}\).
  • Plus seasonality (daily/weekly/annual), equity asymmetry, spread–volume and volume–volatility correlations.
📈What this shows: a normal distribution vs a fat-tailed one with the same variance. The fat-tailed curve is taller in the middle and heavier in the tails (look at the edges) — those tails are the extreme crashes/rallies that a Gaussian random walk badly underestimates.
Analogy

A random walk assumes the weather is mild and independent day to day. Real markets have storm seasons (volatility clusters) and once-a-century floods that happen every decade (fat tails).

Don't say this "Returns are normally distributed." They aren't — they're fat-tailed and show volatility clustering. Assuming normality is exactly what underestimates tail risk (and a flaw behind Black–Scholes' volatility smile).

Market events

How it works

Events are endogenous (from inside — e.g. liquidity shortages triggering cascades) or exogenous (from outside — news).

  • Scheduled news: US Non-Farm Payrolls (first Friday, 8:30am EST), GDP releases, housing/consumption indicators.
  • Unscheduled: the SNB's 2011 EUR/CHF floor, geopolitical shocks, terror attacks.

Both can move prices violently; quantifying their footprint is an active research area.

Analogy

Exogenous events are lightning from outside (news you couldn't predict); endogenous events are the building collapsing under its own weight (a liquidity hole feeding on itself).

Examiner might ask Markets probes
Give two stylised facts and why they break the random-walk model.
Volatility clustering (the random walk assumes constant, independent volatility) and fat tails (the random walk's Gaussian steps make extreme moves astronomically rare, but they happen often).
Market maker vs broker?
A market maker quotes prices, holds inventory, takes risk, and earns the spread. A broker only routes client orders, holds no inventory, and takes no price risk.
Endogenous vs exogenous events — example of each.
Endogenous: a Flash-Crash-style liquidity evaporation. Exogenous: a scheduled NFP surprise or a surprise central-bank decision (SNB floor).
One-line story for the exam "EMH says prices reflect all information (weak/semi-strong/strong) so markets can't be beaten — but bubbles, crashes and especially the stylised facts (volatility clustering, fat tails, scaling laws) show real prices are not a simple random walk, leaving structure that quants try to exploit."
